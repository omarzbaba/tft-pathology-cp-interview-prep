# Project Bank (Part 1)

These ten projects are **adaptable archetypes**, not a résumé. Each one is a fully worked story you can lift, reshape to whatever you have actually done, and deploy when an interviewer says "tell me about a time you improved something in the lab" — the fields below give you a clean 30-second version, a 2-minute version, honest limitations, and a note on how to tell the story credibly even if you contributed to (rather than single-handedly led) the work.

> **How to use this bank.** Do not memorize these as if they were yours — that reads as rehearsed and collapses under a single follow-up. Instead, find the *real* thing in your history that rhymes with each archetype (an LIS tweak you pushed for, a delta-check you helped tune, a TAT problem you sat in a meeting about) and borrow this bank's *structure*: a concrete problem, why it mattered for patients or the lab, who was in the room, what you actually changed, what moved, and what you'd honestly caveat. The **"How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it"** field is the most important one in this whole document — most CPs contribute to these projects rather than own them end to end, and interviewers know that. Owning your real level of contribution honestly is far stronger than inflating it.
>
> **A note on the metrics.** Every number below is *plausible and illustrative*, chosen to be defensible if probed — not a claim about a specific real lab. When you adapt a project, replace these with your real figures or, if you don't have them, say so ("I don't have the exact number in front of me, but the direction was clearly...") rather than inventing precision you can't back up. Fabricated metrics are the fastest way to lose credibility in a technical interview.

## Project 1 — LIS Optimization

**Project title:** Streamlining a cluttered chemistry order-entry and result-review workflow in the LIS.

**Setting:** Core clinical laboratory (high-volume chemistry/immunochemistry) at a community-affiliated hospital running an established LIS with years of accreted build.

**Problem:** Over years of "just add one more," the LIS chemistry build had bloated: duplicate orderable test codes for the same analyte (a legacy "GLUCOSE," a "GLUC-POC," a "GLUCOSE, PLASMA" that all mapped inconsistently), orphaned reflex rules that no longer fired correctly, result-review screens where the most clinically urgent values were not visually prioritized, and free-text comment fields that technologists used inconsistently. Techs were spending extra time hunting for the right orderable, and clinicians occasionally ordered a stale duplicate code that routed oddly.

**Why it mattered:** Cluttered build is not a cosmetic problem — it is a *patient-safety and efficiency* problem. Duplicate codes fragment the longitudinal record (a trend line breaks when the same analyte lives under three codes), confusing review screens slow verification and invite missed flags, and inconsistent comments undermine the downstream clinician reading the report. It also quietly inflates TAT and tech workload at scale.

**Stakeholders:** Laboratory medical director / CP (clinical ownership of test menu and result logic), LIS analyst / lab IT build team (who actually make the changes), lead medical technologists and the bench supervisor (who live in the screens), a representative ordering clinician or two (to sanity-check the orderable menu), and the QA/compliance lead (to keep validation and CAP documentation clean).

**Intervention:** Ran a structured cleanup. Inventoried every chemistry orderable and resultable; identified true duplicates versus legitimately distinct tests; retired or merged redundant codes with a mapping plan so historical data stayed intact and coherent. Rationalized reflex rules (documented each one's clinical intent, killed the dead ones, fixed the miswired ones). Reworked the result-review screen layout so critical and delta-flagged results surfaced first. Standardized a small controlled set of canned comments to replace ad-hoc free text. Every change was validated in a test environment against real historical cases before promotion, and documented for the inspection file.

**Metrics:**
- Number of active chemistry orderables (before vs. after).
- Duplicate/orphaned codes retired.
- Tech time-in-verification per accession (sampled time study, before vs. after).
- Verification-screen "clicks to release" (a proxy for friction).
- Downstream duplicate-order rate from the EHR for consolidated analytes.

**Results:** Reduced active chemistry orderables by roughly a quarter and retired around a dozen dead or duplicate codes. Verification felt materially faster to the bench (sampled time-in-verification down on the order of 10–15%), and the reworked review screen meant flagged results were seen first rather than scrolled past. Duplicate EHR ordering for the consolidated analytes fell off once the stale codes were gone.

**Possible limitations:** Time-study numbers were sampled, not continuously measured, so the efficiency gain is directional rather than precise. Retiring codes required careful data-mapping to avoid orphaning historical results — one legacy report format needed rework mid-project. Clinician-facing benefit (fewer wrong-code orders) is real but hard to attribute cleanly because ordering behavior has many drivers. And build cleanup is never "done" — it decays again without governance.

**30-second description:** "Our chemistry LIS build had accumulated years of clutter — duplicate test codes, dead reflex rules, and a result-review screen that didn't prioritize the urgent stuff. I led a cleanup: inventoried everything, retired or merged redundant codes without losing historical data, fixed the reflex logic, and reorganized the review screen so flagged results surfaced first. Verification got noticeably faster and duplicate ordering dropped."

**2-minute explanation:** "Like a lot of established labs, our chemistry LIS build had grown by accretion — every new need added a code or a rule, and nobody ever pruned. So we had three orderables that all meant 'glucose,' reflex rules that no longer fired correctly, and a verification screen where a critical potassium and a normal chloride looked visually identical. That's not cosmetic — it fragments the patient's trend line across duplicate codes, it slows the techs, and it risks a flag getting scrolled past. I partnered with our LIS analyst, the bench supervisor, and QA to run a disciplined cleanup. We inventoried every orderable and resultable, separated true duplicates from legitimately distinct tests, and retired or merged the redundant ones — with a careful data-mapping plan so historical results stayed coherent and a broken trend line didn't appear. We documented the clinical intent of every reflex rule, killed the dead ones, and fixed the miswired ones. We reworked the review screen so critical and delta-flagged results sort to the top. Everything was validated against real historical cases in a test environment before it went live, and documented for CAP. The net effect: about a quarter fewer active orderables, verification measurably faster on a sampled time study, and duplicate EHR ordering down for the analytes we consolidated. The honest caveat is that build cleanup decays — so I also set up a lightweight governance step so new codes get reviewed instead of just added."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "I treated the LIS build like a codebase that needed refactoring — same functionality, far less clutter, and a rule you could actually reason about. The discipline was in the data mapping and the validation, not the deleting."

**Competency demonstrated:** LIS domain fluency; test-menu governance; data-integrity thinking (protecting historical continuity); collaboration with lab IT and the bench; validation and documentation discipline; result-presentation as a patient-safety concern.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you were a contributor, say so plainly: "I wasn't the project owner, but I was the CP the analyst brought clinical questions to — I made the calls on which reflex rules were clinically necessary, which codes were truly duplicate versus distinct, and how the review screen should prioritize flags." That's a genuinely valuable role and it demonstrates the exact competency they care about (clinical judgment applied to the build) without overclaiming. If your real experience is smaller — even just noticing and flagging a duplicate-code problem — frame it as pattern recognition: "I've learned to spot LIS build decay, and here's what I'd do about it."

## Project 2 — Middleware Cleanup

**Project title:** Rationalizing an overgrown middleware rule set between the chemistry analyzers and the LIS.

**Setting:** Core lab with instrument middleware (the data-manager layer) sitting between the automated chemistry/immunoassay line and the LIS, handling QC, delta checks, reflexes, and autoverification rules.

**Problem:** The middleware rule set had become a thicket. Over time, rules had been layered on to handle one-off situations — a rule to hold results on a specific instrument, a rule to add a comment for a specific ordering location, redundant delta-check rules that overlapped and sometimes conflicted, and rules referencing analyzers that had since been retired. Nobody had a current, authoritative map of *why* each rule existed. The symptom: results occasionally held for non-obvious reasons, techs manually overriding holds they didn't understand, and no one confident enough to touch the rules for fear of breaking something.

**Why it mattered:** Middleware is where a lot of the real intelligence lives — delta checks, critical flags, reflexes, and autoverification often execute *before* a result ever reaches the LIS. An opaque, conflicting rule set means results are held or released for reasons no one can explain, which is both an efficiency drain and a latent safety risk. Manual overrides of holds nobody understands is exactly how a genuinely dangerous result eventually slips through.

**Stakeholders:** CP / medical director (owns the clinical logic and is accountable for what autoverifies), middleware administrator / lab IT (executes and version-controls the rules), bench techs and supervisor (who feel the holds), QC/QA lead (validation and documentation), and the instrument vendor's field application specialist (for platform-specific rule behavior).

**Intervention:** Built an authoritative inventory of every active rule with its clinical or operational intent, owner, and last-reviewed date — essentially documentation that never existed. Identified conflicting and redundant delta-check and hold rules and consolidated them into a coherent, non-overlapping set. Removed rules referencing retired instruments and defunct locations. For each surviving rule, re-derived and re-validated the threshold against current clinical logic and reference intervals. Established a change-control process so future rule changes are proposed, reviewed, tested, and documented rather than added ad hoc. Critically, we did this incrementally and monitored hold/override rates after each batch of changes rather than doing a risky big-bang cutover.

**Metrics:**
- Total active rules (before vs. after).
- Conflicting/redundant rules resolved.
- Unexplained-hold rate and manual-override rate (before vs. after).
- Autoverification pass-through rate (should rise as noise rules are cleaned).
- Presence of complete, current rule documentation (binary: existed / didn't).

**Results:** Cut the active rule count meaningfully (on the order of 20–30%), eliminated the conflicting delta rules that had caused mystery holds, and — importantly — produced a documented, reviewable rule set where previously there was tribal knowledge. Manual overrides of holds dropped as techs stopped encountering holds they couldn't explain, and autoverification pass-through improved modestly because we stopped tripping on redundant rules. The biggest win was arguably qualitative: the team regained the confidence to *touch* the middleware safely.

**Possible limitations:** The efficiency gains are real but modest — middleware cleanup mostly *prevents* problems (a dangerous override, an unexplained delay) rather than producing a dramatic headline number, which makes it a harder project to "sell" with metrics. Re-validating every threshold is labor-intensive. And the incremental, monitored approach is slower than a big cutover — deliberately, because safety came first.

**30-second description:** "Our chemistry middleware had years of layered-on rules — conflicting delta checks, holds nobody could explain, rules pointing at retired instruments. I led a cleanup: documented every rule's actual purpose, consolidated the conflicting ones, retired the dead ones, and put change-control in place. Mystery holds and unexplained overrides dropped, and for the first time we had a rule set we could actually reason about safely."

**2-minute explanation:** "Middleware is the layer where the real decision logic lives — delta checks, critical flags, reflexes, autoverification — and ours had become a black box. Rules had been added one at a time over years to solve individual problems, and nobody had ever written down why each one existed. The result was that results would sometimes hold for reasons the techs couldn't explain, so they'd override the hold — and overriding a hold you don't understand is exactly how a truly dangerous result eventually gets out. I ran a structured cleanup. First, an inventory: every active rule, its clinical or operational intent, its owner, when it was last reviewed. That documentation had literally never existed. Then we found the conflicting and redundant delta rules and consolidated them into a clean, non-overlapping set, retired rules that referenced instruments we no longer ran, and re-validated the threshold on every surviving rule against current clinical logic. We did it incrementally, monitoring hold and override rates after each batch rather than a risky big-bang change. And we stood up a change-control process so this never re-rots — new rules get proposed, reviewed, tested, and documented. We cut the rule count by 20-plus percent, killed the mystery holds, and improved autoverification pass-through. But honestly the biggest win was that the team could finally touch the middleware without fear, because we understood it again."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "The dangerous thing wasn't the number of rules — it was that no one could explain them, so techs were overriding holds blindly. I made the rule set legible and accountable again, which matters more than any single efficiency number."

**Competency demonstrated:** Deep understanding of where autoverification logic actually executes; risk-aware change management (incremental, monitored); governance and documentation discipline; recognizing a latent safety hazard that isn't showing up as an incident *yet*; collaboration with vendors and bench staff.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If your role was clinical consultation rather than ownership: "The middleware admin ran the mechanics, but I was the physician who decided which delta thresholds and hold rules were clinically justified and which were noise — that's the judgment call that makes or breaks the cleanup." If you've only observed the problem, frame it as informed diagnosis: "I've seen middleware rot become a safety issue when techs override holds they can't explain — so my instinct on any middleware is: is there current documentation of *why* each rule exists, and if not, that's job one." Interviewers respect a CP who understands the middleware layer at all — most underestimate it.

## Project 3 — Autoverification Refinement

**Project title:** Tightening and expanding chemistry autoverification to catch more, release faster, and hold only what needs a human.

**Setting:** High-volume core lab where routine chemistry results pass through autoverification rules; a mix of over-holding (too many normal results kicked to techs) and a few under-specified rules (edge cases that shouldn't have auto-released).

**Problem:** The autoverification rule set was both too loose and too tight in different places. Too *tight*: overly conservative rules were holding large volumes of unremarkable results for manual review, burning tech time on results that never needed a human. Too *loose*: a few rules lacked adequate delta-check or absurdity guardrails, so a physiologically implausible value or a specimen-integrity issue (hemolysis, contamination) could occasionally auto-release. The rule logic also hadn't been formally re-validated against current method performance and reference intervals in some time.

**Why it mattered:** Autoverification done well is one of the highest-leverage things a core lab can do — it releases most results in seconds without a human touch, freeing techs to focus on the results that genuinely need judgment, and it shortens TAT. But it is only safe if the rules are correct and validated. An autoverification rule that releases a hemolyzed potassium or a delta-check failure is a direct patient-safety event. The goal is *more* automation with *tighter* guardrails — not a tradeoff between the two.

**Stakeholders:** CP / medical director (owns and signs off the rules, accountable to CAP/CLIA), lead techs and bench supervisor (define real-world edge cases), middleware/LIS administrator (implements), QA (validation study design and documentation), and the informatics or quality committee that reviews the go-live.

**Intervention:** Re-derived the rule logic from first principles: for each analyte, defined the autoverifiable range, required delta-check behavior, specimen-integrity checks (hemolysis/icterus/lipemia indices), QC status dependencies, and instrument-flag handling. Loosened the over-conservative holds where the data showed nothing was being caught, and tightened or added guardrails where edge cases had slipped. Designed a validation study: ran the proposed rules in parallel/silent mode against a large set of real recent results, compared auto-release decisions against tech decisions, and reviewed every discordance before turning rules on. Documented the whole thing for the inspection file, and set a schedule for periodic rule re-review.

**Metrics:**
- Autoverification pass-through rate (% released without human touch) before vs. after.
- Manual-review volume per shift (tech workload).
- TAT for autoverifiable results.
- False-release events / discordances caught in the validation study (safety metric).
- Rule documentation and re-validation cadence established.

**Results:** Raised the autoverification pass-through rate substantially (for example, from roughly 60% to the mid-80s% on routine chemistry), which cut the manual-review queue and shortened TAT for straightforward results — while the validation study confirmed the tighter guardrails caught the specimen-integrity and delta edge cases that had previously been at risk of auto-releasing. Techs redirected their attention to genuinely abnormal or complex results.

**Possible limitations:** The exact pass-through gain depends heavily on the analyte mix and how conservative the starting rules were — a lab already at 80% won't see a jump like this. The validation study is labor-intensive and requires a large, representative result set to be trustworthy. And autoverification always carries residual risk: no rule set catches everything, so the honest framing is "safer and faster," not "risk-free." Ongoing monitoring is non-negotiable.

**30-second description:** "Our chemistry autoverification was simultaneously too loose and too tight — holding tons of normal results for no reason while occasionally auto-releasing edge cases like a hemolyzed sample. I refined the rules: loosened the pointless holds, tightened the guardrails on delta checks and specimen integrity, and validated everything in silent mode against real results before go-live. Pass-through went up, TAT went down, and the dangerous edge cases got caught."

**2-minute explanation:** "Autoverification is one of the highest-leverage things a core lab does — it releases the vast majority of results in seconds without a human, which frees the techs to focus on the results that actually need judgment and it shortens turnaround. But it's only safe if the rules are right. Ours had a split personality. In some places it was far too conservative — holding huge volumes of perfectly normal results for manual review, which was pure wasted tech time. In other places it was too loose — a few rules didn't have adequate delta-check or specimen-integrity guardrails, so a hemolyzed potassium or a physiologically implausible value could occasionally auto-release, which is a real safety problem. So I re-derived the rule logic analyte by analyte: the autoverifiable range, the required delta behavior, the hemolysis/icterus/lipemia checks, QC dependencies, instrument flags. Where the data showed a hold was catching nothing, we loosened it. Where edge cases had slipped, we added guardrails. Then — and this is the important part — we didn't just flip a switch. We ran the proposed rules in silent mode against a large set of recent real results, compared what the rules *would* have done against what the techs actually did, and reviewed every single discordance before go-live. All documented for CAP, with a re-review schedule. The result was pass-through up into the mid-80s from around 60%, manual review volume down, TAT down for routine results, and — validated, not assumed — the dangerous edge cases now getting held. The honest caveat is that no rule set is perfect, so this is 'faster and safer,' not 'risk-free,' and it needs ongoing monitoring."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "The goal wasn't to automate more *or* be safer — it was both at once. More automation with tighter guardrails, proven by running the rules silently against real data before I trusted them with a patient's result."

**Competency demonstrated:** Autoverification as a core-lab lever; validation-study design (silent/parallel mode, discordance review); balancing efficiency against safety rather than trading one for the other; regulatory documentation; ongoing-monitoring mindset; direct connection between a rule and a patient outcome.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you participated in validating or signing off rules rather than designing the whole study: "I was one of the pathologists who reviewed the silent-mode discordances and made the release/hold calls on the edge cases — that clinical adjudication is the safety-critical piece." If your exposure is conceptual, be honest and show you understand the method: "I haven't personally built an autoverification study, but I understand exactly how it should be done — silent mode against real results, discordance review before go-live, documented and monitored — and I'd want to be the clinical owner of those rules." Understanding the *validation logic* is what they're testing more than authorship.

## Project 4 — TAT Dashboard

**Project title:** Building a turnaround-time dashboard that decomposes TAT into its actual bottleneck stages.

**Setting:** Core lab under pressure from the ED and inpatient services over "slow" stat results, with only crude, aggregate TAT reporting available.

**Problem:** The lab reported TAT as a single order-to-result number, which hid *where* the time actually went. When the ED complained that troponin was slow, no one could say whether the delay was pre-analytic (collection, tube transport, receipt/accessioning), analytic (instrument load, repeats, QC holds), or post-analytic (verification, release, result availability in the EHR). Improvement efforts were guesses. The aggregate number also averaged away the outliers — the 2% of specimens with a 3-hour TAT that were driving the complaints.

**Why it mattered:** TAT is where informatics meets lived clinical reality — a delayed troponin or potassium delays a real decision at a real bedside. You cannot fix a turnaround problem you can't decompose; a single averaged number both hides the bottleneck and hides the outliers, which are exactly the specimens generating clinician frustration and, occasionally, patient harm. A stage-resolved dashboard turns "the lab is slow" into "receipt-to-verification for stat troponin from the ED has a tail at shift change," which is actionable.

**Stakeholders:** CP / lab leadership (owns the definition of TAT and the improvement priorities), LIS analyst / data or informatics team (build the extract and the dashboard), ED and inpatient clinical leaders (define what "on time" means to them and validate the targets), bench supervisors (interpret what the stage timestamps mean operationally), and phlebotomy/transport leads (own the pre-analytic segment that labs often can't see).

**Intervention:** Defined TAT properly as a set of timestamped segments — collection → receipt → in-instrument → resulted → verified → available in EHR — and confirmed which timestamps the LIS actually captured reliably (a real constraint; collection time is often manually entered and unreliable). Built a dashboard that showed each segment separately, broken out by priority (stat vs. routine), location (ED vs. floors), analyte (troponin, potassium, lactate), and shift. Reported the 90th percentile alongside the median so the outlier tail was visible instead of averaged away. Used the dashboard to target the actual bottleneck — which turned out to be receipt-to-verification during shift-change windows — rather than blaming the instrument.

**Metrics:**
- Stage-resolved TAT (median and 90th percentile) per segment.
- Stat troponin/potassium TAT, ED vs. inpatient.
- % of stat results meeting the agreed target.
- Outlier count (results exceeding the tail threshold) per week.
- Whether improvement efforts could now be targeted (qualitative but real).

**Results:** The dashboard immediately relocated the problem: the delay wasn't the analyzer, it was a receipt/verification backlog at shift change. Targeting that window (staggered breaks, a verification-coverage adjustment) cut the 90th-percentile stat troponin TAT noticeably and raised the share of stat results meeting target. Equally important, the ED conversation changed from "you're slow" to a shared, data-grounded discussion — the dashboard became the meeting's common reference.

**Possible limitations:** The dashboard is only as good as the timestamps feeding it, and pre-analytic timestamps (especially collection time) are frequently unreliable, so the earliest segment is the weakest data. The median-vs-90th-percentile framing helps but doesn't fix a fundamentally noisy input. And a dashboard *reveals* problems — it doesn't fix them; the improvement came from the operational change it enabled, not from the dashboard itself. Sustained benefit needs someone to actually watch it.

**30-second description:** "The ED kept saying stat labs were slow, but we only had a single aggregate TAT number that hid where the time went. I built a dashboard that broke TAT into its real stages — receipt, analysis, verification, EHR availability — split by priority and location, showing the 90th percentile so outliers weren't averaged away. It showed the bottleneck was verification at shift change, not the instrument, and once we fixed that the stat TAT tail dropped."

**2-minute explanation:** "TAT is where informatics meets the bedside — a slow troponin delays a real decision. The problem was that we reported TAT as one number, order-to-result, which does two bad things: it hides *where* the time goes, and it averages away the outliers, which are exactly the specimens the ED is angry about. So when they complained troponin was slow, we genuinely couldn't say whether it was collection, transport, accessioning, the instrument, or verification. Improvement was guesswork. I worked with our data team to define TAT properly as a chain of timestamped segments and to figure out which timestamps the LIS actually captured reliably — which matters, because collection time is often manually entered and untrustworthy, so I was honest that the earliest segment was our weakest data. Then we built a dashboard that showed each segment separately, broken out by stat versus routine, by ED versus floors, by analyte, and by shift — and it reported the 90th percentile next to the median so the outlier tail was visible. That immediately relocated the problem: the delay wasn't the analyzer at all, it was a receipt-and-verification backlog at shift change. We addressed that with staggered breaks and a coverage tweak, and the 90th-percentile stat troponin TAT came down meaningfully. But the subtler win was that the ED conversation changed. Instead of 'you're slow' versus 'no we're not,' we had one shared dashboard on the screen, and the discussion became collaborative. The caveat I'd always give: a dashboard reveals the problem, it doesn't fix it — and it's only as trustworthy as the timestamps underneath it."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "A single TAT average is worse than useless because it hides both the bottleneck and the outliers. The value was decomposing it into stages and showing the 90th percentile — that's what turned 'the lab is slow' into a specific, fixable problem."

**Competency demonstrated:** Analytics and dashboard design in service of operations; understanding the total testing process (pre/analytic/post); statistical literacy (percentiles vs. means, outliers); data-quality honesty (unreliable timestamps); using data to convert an adversarial clinician relationship into a collaborative one.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If the data team built it and you specified it: "I didn't write the query, but I defined what TAT actually means segment by segment, insisted we show the 90th percentile not just the average, and interpreted what the shift-change bottleneck meant operationally — the clinical and operational framing was mine." If you've mostly *used* such a dashboard: "I'm a heavy user of stage-resolved TAT data — I know to look at the tail, not the mean, and to ask which segment owns the delay before assigning blame." Even naming the pre-analytic timestamp reliability problem unprompted signals real operational maturity.

## Project 5 — Quality Dashboard

**Project title:** A laboratory quality dashboard consolidating QC, proficiency testing, error events, and key quality indicators into one operational view.

**Setting:** Core lab where quality data lived in silos — QC in the middleware, proficiency testing (PT) in binders and vendor portals, incident reports in a separate safety system, and quality indicators tracked (if at all) in scattered spreadsheets.

**Problem:** No one had a single, current view of the lab's quality state. QC trends, PT performance, corrected-report rates, specimen-rejection rates, critical-value callback compliance, and TAT-quality indicators all lived in different places, updated on different schedules, and were assembled painfully by hand for monthly QA meetings and CAP inspection prep. Problems were caught late — a drifting QC trend or a slipping critical-callback rate might not surface until the monthly review, or until an inspector asked.

**Why it mattered:** Quality in a clinical lab is not a monthly report — it's a continuous state that either is or isn't under control right now. Fragmented quality data means slow detection: a Westgard-flag pattern, a PT miss, or a falling critical-value documentation rate is a signal you want to see *this week*, not next month. It also means inspection prep is a fire drill instead of a byproduct of daily work. A consolidated dashboard shortens the loop between a quality signal and a human noticing it.

**Stakeholders:** CP / medical director and QA/quality manager (own the quality program and the indicators that matter), LIS/informatics team (aggregate the data sources), bench supervisors (act on the signals), point-of-care and section leads (contribute their indicators), and — in the background — the CAP/CLIA accreditation requirements that define many of these metrics.

**Intervention:** Selected a focused set of quality indicators that actually drive action rather than a vanity wall of numbers — QC out-of-control and rejection rates by instrument, PT performance and any misses, corrected-report (amended result) rate, specimen rejection/hemolysis rates, critical-value callback documentation compliance, and a couple of key TAT indicators. Pulled these from their source systems into one dashboard with clear thresholds and visual flags (green/amber/red), refreshed on a cadence fast enough to be operational (e.g., daily/weekly, not monthly). Built it so the CAP-relevant indicators mapped directly to inspection requirements, making prep a matter of pulling a view rather than assembling binders.

**Metrics:**
- Time-to-detection for a quality signal (how fast a drift/miss is noticed) — before vs. after.
- Critical-value callback documentation compliance rate.
- Corrected-report (amended result) rate trend.
- Specimen rejection / hemolysis rate trend.
- Inspection-prep effort (hours to assemble quality documentation).

**Results:** The lab caught quality signals earlier — a drifting QC pattern or a dip in critical-callback documentation surfaced in the weekly view instead of the monthly meeting, so corrective action started sooner. Critical-value callback documentation compliance improved once it was visible and owned. Inspection preparation went from a multi-day scramble to largely a byproduct of the existing dashboard. The QA meeting itself got shorter and sharper because everyone walked in already looking at the same current picture.

**Possible limitations:** A dashboard can create a false sense of coverage — it shows the indicators you chose, and quality problems outside that set are still invisible, so indicator selection is a real ongoing judgment. Some sources (especially manual processes and paper-based POCT) resist automated aggregation and still needed manual feeds. And, as with any dashboard, it detects and displays but doesn't act — the improvement came from people responding to the signals, which requires that someone actually owns each red flag.

**30-second description:** "Our quality data was scattered — QC in one system, PT in binders, incidents in another, indicators in spreadsheets — so problems surfaced late, usually at the monthly meeting or when an inspector asked. I consolidated the metrics that actually drive action into one dashboard with clear thresholds, refreshed weekly. We started catching drifts and compliance dips early, critical-callback documentation improved, and CAP prep stopped being a fire drill."

**2-minute explanation:** "Quality in a lab isn't a monthly report — it's a state that either is or isn't under control right now. But our quality data was completely fragmented: QC lived in the middleware, proficiency testing in binders and vendor portals, incident reports in the safety system, quality indicators in whatever spreadsheet someone maintained. So a drifting QC trend or a slipping critical-value callback rate might not surface until the monthly QA meeting — or until a CAP inspector asked, at which point it's a fire drill. I built a consolidated quality dashboard, and the key discipline was choosing indicators that actually drive action rather than putting up a vanity wall of numbers. So: QC out-of-control and rejection rates by instrument, PT performance and misses, the amended-result rate, specimen rejection and hemolysis rates, critical-value callback documentation compliance, a couple of key TAT quality indicators. We pulled these into one view with green/amber/red thresholds, refreshed fast enough to be operational — weekly, not monthly. And I deliberately mapped the CAP-relevant indicators to their inspection requirements, so prep became pulling a view instead of assembling binders. The payoff was earlier detection — signals surfaced in the weekly view, so corrective action started sooner — and critical-callback documentation compliance actually improved once it was visible and owned by name. Inspection prep went from a multi-day scramble to a byproduct of daily work, and the QA meeting got sharper because everyone was already looking at the same current picture. ## Project 6 — Utilization Review

**Project title:** A test-utilization initiative to curb inappropriate and redundant send-out and high-cost testing.

**Setting:** Health-system lab where clinicians could order essentially any test with minimal friction, driving redundant repeat testing and inappropriate high-cost / send-out orders.

**Problem:** Utilization data showed classic overuse patterns: daily inpatient vitamin D and ferritin repeats with no plausible clinical change, duplicate ordering of the same test within a single admission, high-cost genetic/molecular send-outs ordered without the specialty consult that guidelines call for, and expensive tests ordered when a cheaper first-line test hadn't been done. The lab absorbed the cost and workload; occasionally a redundant blood draw contributed to iatrogenic anemia.

**Why it mattered:** Test utilization is a governance responsibility that sits squarely with the CP as steward of the test menu — it's "right test, right time, no waste." Overuse costs money, but the more compelling frame is patient care: unnecessary phlebotomy contributes to hospital-acquired anemia, false-positive results from low-pretest-probability testing trigger downstream workups, and send-out backlog delays the tests that genuinely matter. It's also a place where the CP earns credibility with clinicians by improving care, not just cutting cost.

**Stakeholders:** CP / laboratory director (owns the test menu and CDS logic), a physician champion or the relevant clinical service leaders (utilization change lives or dies on clinician buy-in), pharmacy and utilization/value committees, the EHR/informatics team (build the order-entry and CDS changes), lab finance (quantify send-out spend), and — essential — the ordering clinicians themselves, who must not feel the lab is simply blocking care.

**Intervention:** Started with data, not restrictions: pulled utilization patterns to identify the highest-yield targets (a handful of tests, not a crusade against everything). For redundant repeats, implemented soft stops in order entry — a duplicate-order alert or a minimum-retest-interval rule (e.g., "vitamin D was resulted 3 days ago; reorder?"). For high-cost send-outs, added a gentle CDS prompt or a requirement for genetics/specialty involvement per guideline. Framed everything as decision support, not hard blocks, to preserve clinician autonomy and avoid the backlash that kills utilization programs. Partnered with a physician champion to socialize the changes. Monitored both the utilization drop *and* any unintended clinical consequences.

**Metrics:**
- Order volume for targeted tests (before vs. after).
- Duplicate/redundant order rate within an admission.
- Send-out spend for targeted high-cost tests.
- % of high-cost molecular orders with appropriate specialty involvement.
- Safety monitor: any evidence of under-testing / missed appropriate orders (guardrail metric).

**Results:** Meaningful reductions on the targeted tests — redundant repeats fell once the soft-stop retest-interval alerts were in place, and high-cost send-out volume for the targeted assays dropped with the CDS prompts, producing real cost savings without hard blocks. Just as important, the safety monitor showed no signal of appropriate tests being suppressed. The soft-stop framing meant clinicians experienced it as help, not obstruction, which preserved the relationship for the next initiative.

**Possible limitations:** Soft stops are, by design, overridable — so they reduce rather than eliminate overuse, and alert fatigue is a genuine risk if you deploy too many. Attributing cost savings cleanly is hard because ordering patterns shift for many reasons. And utilization gains erode over time (staff turnover, new ordering habits) without periodic reinforcement. The initiative also only works if you pick a small number of high-yield targets — trying to fix everything at once fails.

**30-second description:** "Our utilization data showed classic overuse — daily vitamin D and ferritin repeats, duplicate orders in one admission, expensive send-outs without the specialty consult guidelines call for. Redundant testing and send-out spend dropped, with no signal that appropriate tests were being suppressed."

## Project 7 — Digital Pathology Validation

**Project title:** Validating a whole-slide imaging system for primary diagnosis in a defined use case.

**Setting:** Department standing up whole-slide imaging (WSI) — scanners, an image management system, viewers — moving from glass-only toward digital review, requiring a formal validation before any digital primary diagnosis.

**Problem:** The department wanted to use whole-slide images for primary diagnosis (not just archiving or teaching), which cannot be done by simply buying scanners and trusting them. Validation is required to demonstrate that a pathologist reaches the *same diagnosis* on the digital image as on the glass slide, across the intended case mix, with the specific scanner/viewer/display configuration that will be used clinically. Without a rigorous, documented validation, digital primary diagnosis is neither defensible nor safe.

**Why it mattered:** A whole-slide image is not an ordinary photograph — it's a gigapixel, multi-resolution, color-managed file, and its diagnostic adequacy depends on the entire chain: scanner, focus, resolution, color, the viewer, and the display. Validation is the governance act that makes digital diagnosis trustworthy: it protects patients from a missed diagnosis caused by a scanning artifact or an inadequate display, and it satisfies CAP expectations for WSI validation. Getting this right is exactly the kind of digital-pathology judgment a modern CP is expected to have.

**Stakeholders:** Pathologists (AP colleagues who will read digitally and who participate as validation readers), the CP/informatics lead coordinating the validation design and documentation, histology (slide and scan quality), lab IT / vendor (scanner, IMS, viewer, display specs), and the quality/compliance lead (mapping the study to CAP's WSI validation guidance).

**Intervention:** Designed the validation per the recognized framework (CAP's WSI validation guidance): assembled a representative case set spanning the intended diagnostic use, including harder and edge cases, at an adequate sample size. Had pathologists review each case on glass and on the digital image with a washout period between the two reads to avoid recall bias. Compared digital-vs-glass diagnoses for concordance, adjudicated every discordance to understand whether it was a true digital limitation, a scanning/focus artifact, or ordinary interobserver variation. Locked and documented the exact validated configuration — scanner, magnification, viewer, display — because validation is tied to that specific chain. Documented the whole study for the inspection file and defined the scope of what was (and wasn't) validated for clinical use.

**Metrics:**
- Digital-vs-glass diagnostic concordance rate (primary endpoint).
- Number and nature of discordances (true digital limitation vs. artifact vs. interobserver variation).
- Washout period observed (methodological integrity).
- Case-set size and representativeness against the intended use.
- Validated configuration locked and documented (scope statement).

**Results:** Achieved a high digital-glass concordance across the case set, with the small number of discordances adjudicating mostly to ordinary interobserver variation rather than a digital-specific failure — with a couple of scanning/focus artifacts identified that fed back into a histology/scanning QC step. The output was a defensible, documented validation supporting digital primary diagnosis for the defined scope, plus a clear statement of what remained out of scope (e.g., certain stains or specimen types deferred to a later validation).

**Possible limitations:** Validation is specific to the validated use case and configuration — it does not license "digital pathology" in general, so scope discipline is essential and easy to overstate. A washout period and adequate case set make the study slow and resource-intensive. Concordance metrics are sensitive to case mix (an easy case set inflates concordance), so representativeness is a real methodological risk. And validation is a point-in-time act; a scanner change, viewer upgrade, or display swap can invalidate it and require revalidation.

**30-second description:** "We were moving to whole-slide imaging for primary diagnosis, which requires formal validation — you have to prove a pathologist reaches the same diagnosis on the digital image as on glass. I helped design the validation per CAP's framework: a representative case set, glass-versus-digital reads with a washout period to avoid recall bias, concordance measured, every discordance adjudicated, and the exact scanner/viewer/display configuration locked and documented. We got high concordance and a defensible validation for the defined scope."

**2-minute explanation:** "A whole-slide image isn't a photograph — it's a gigapixel, multi-resolution, color-managed file, and its diagnostic adequacy depends on the whole chain: scanner, focus, resolution, color, the viewer, and the display. So you can't just buy scanners and start signing out digitally; you have to validate that a pathologist reaches the same diagnosis on the image as on the glass, for the specific case mix and the specific hardware you'll use clinically. That's a governance act, and it's what makes digital diagnosis safe and defensible. I helped design the validation around CAP's WSI validation guidance. The core methodology: assemble a representative case set — and 'representative' is the crux, because an easy case set inflates your concordance and lies to you, so we deliberately included harder and edge cases across the intended use. This is a great archetype to show that validation *logic* is a portable competency.

## Project 8 — Standardization Across Sites

**Project title:** Standardizing reference intervals and result reporting for shared analytes across multiple sites in a health system.

**Setting:** Multi-site health system (the BILH-type scenario) where several hospital labs, brought together over time, ran the same analytes on different instruments with different reference intervals, units, and result formats.

**Problem:** The same test meant different things at different sites. A creatinine reference interval differed between two hospitals; a result reported in one unit at one site and another unit elsewhere; a coagulation result formatted differently; the same analyte carrying different flags. For a patient moving between sites — or a clinician covering multiple facilities — this was genuinely dangerous: a value flagged abnormal at one hospital looked normal at another, and trended results were incomparable across the very system that was supposed to be integrated.

**Why it mattered:** In a growing, consolidating system, standardization is the difference between "several labs" and "one lab service." Discordant reference intervals and formats aren't a tidiness problem — they're a patient-safety and continuity-of-care problem: a clinician comparing a creatinine trend across two sites can be misled, and a covering physician can misread a flag. It also blocks system-wide analytics, antibiograms, and shared decision support, all of which assume comparable data. This is precisely the integration work a CP in a growing system is hired to help lead.

**Stakeholders:** System lab leadership / medical directors across sites (must agree on the harmonized standards), the section technical leads and QA (who validate that standardized intervals are valid for each instrument), the informatics/LIS team (implement the standardized build across instances), clinical stakeholders (who need to understand what's changing and why), and the accreditation lead (documentation across sites).

**Intervention:** Approached it as harmonization, not blunt imposition — because a reference interval is tied to the *method*, you can't just decree one number across different instruments without confirming it's valid on each. For each shared analyte: compared methods and instruments across sites, determined where a single harmonized reference interval was analytically justified (comparable methods) versus where a method difference genuinely required a documented, method-specific interval. Standardized units to a common convention (ideally UCUM-coded) and standardized result formatting and flagging where clinically appropriate. Where a truly harmonized interval wasn't valid, made the difference explicit and documented rather than silent. Ran the changes through a governance process across sites, validated per instrument, and communicated the changes to clinicians before go-live. Sequenced the rollout site by site rather than all at once.

**Metrics:**
- Number of shared analytes harmonized to a common reference interval / unit / format.
- Number requiring documented method-specific intervals (honest denominator).
- Cross-site result-format discrepancies remaining.
- Clinician-reported confusion / safety events related to cross-site result interpretation.
- System-wide analytics enabled (e.g., a unified antibiogram or comparable trending) — qualitative.

**Results:** Harmonized a large share of high-volume shared analytes to common reference intervals, units, and formats, so a creatinine or a CBC read the same across sites and trended coherently for a patient who moved between them — while honestly documenting the minority of analytes where a genuine method difference required a site-specific interval rather than pretending it away. This unlocked comparable cross-site trending and laid the groundwork for system-wide analytics.

**Possible limitations:** You cannot standardize away real analytical differences — where methods genuinely differ, a single reference interval is wrong and unsafe, so a subset legitimately stays method-specific, and pretending otherwise is the dangerous failure mode. The work is slow and politically delicate because it requires medical directors at multiple sites to cede local convention. Validating harmonized intervals per instrument is real work. And clinician communication is essential — a silently changed reference interval erodes trust fast.

## Project 9 — Test Menu Harmonization

**Project title:** Consolidating and harmonizing the orderable test menu and its coding across newly integrated laboratories.

When the labs needed to interface or share an LIS, this catalog chaos meant orders didn't route cleanly, results didn't aggregate, and system-wide reporting and analytics were impossible. It also created clinician confusion when providers worked across sites.

**Why it mattered:** The test menu is the front door of the lab — if two sites call the same test different things and code it differently, nothing downstream works cleanly: interfaces break, results fragment across codes, LOINC-based interoperability fails, and any system-wide analytics or decision support built on the data is unreliable. Sequenced the rollout and kept a mapping table so historical orders remained interpretable.

- % of shared orderables correctly mapped to LOINC.
- Panels reconciled to consistent, clinically vetted definitions.
- Clean order-routing and result-aggregation success across the interface (before vs. after).
- Billing/order-catalog reconciliation completeness.

## Project 10 — Interface Troubleshooting

**Project title:** Diagnosing and resolving an intermittent LIS↔EHR interface failure causing missing and mismatched results.

**Setting:** Core lab with a bidirectional HL7 interface between the LIS and the EHR (Epic-type environment), where results were intermittently not appearing in the EHR or appearing attached to the wrong context.

**Problem:** Clinicians reported that some results weren't showing up in the EHR, and occasionally a result appeared under the wrong order or with a delay. It was intermittent, which is the worst kind — it wasn't a total outage that everyone notices, it was a slow leak that eroded trust and, more importantly, risked a clinician making a decision on incomplete data or, in the worst case, missing a critical result entirely. The root cause wasn't obvious: it could be at the LIS, in the interface engine, in the HL7 message content, or on the EHR side.

**Why it mattered:** An interface is the pipe between the lab and the clinician, and a leaky pipe is a direct patient-safety hazard — a result that never reaches the EHR is, from the bedside, a result that doesn't exist. Intermittent interface problems are especially dangerous because they don't trigger the alarms a full outage does; they silently drop or misroute a fraction of results. Being able to reason systematically across the LIS → interface engine → HL7 message → EHR chain is exactly the informatics-troubleshooting fluency this role wants, because in a multi-site system the number and complexity of interfaces only grows.

**Stakeholders:** CP / lab leadership (owns the clinical impact and prioritization), the interface analyst / integration-engine team (read the message logs, trace the transactions), LIS and EHR analysts (both ends of the pipe), the clinicians reporting the issue (define the pattern), and the vendor(s) if the defect sits in their software.

**Intervention:** Treated it as a systematic trace, not a guess. Established the pattern first (which analytes, which locations, which times — intermittent problems almost always have a pattern that points to the cause). Pulled the interface-engine transaction logs to determine *where* in the chain messages were being dropped or malformed — did the LIS send the ORU, did the engine receive and route it, did the EHR acknowledge it? Inspected the actual HL7 message content for the failing cases and found the tell: certain results carried a character or field-length condition (an unexpected special character / a specimen-comment field exceeding what the EHR interface accepted) that caused the EHR to reject or mishandle the message without an obvious error surfacing to the lab. Worked with the interface team and vendor to fix the message handling, added monitoring/alerting so silent drops would surface as an alert rather than a clinician complaint, and reconciled the affected historical results to ensure none were clinically stranded.

**Metrics:**
- Rate of missing/mismatched results in the EHR (before vs. after).
- Time-to-detection for interface message failures (added monitoring).
- Number of historical affected results identified and reconciled (safety closure).
- Interface message acknowledgment / rejection rate.
- Clinician-reported interface incidents post-fix.

**Results:** Isolated the root cause to a specific HL7 message-content condition that the EHR interface silently rejected, fixed the message handling so those results transmitted correctly, and — importantly — reconciled the historical affected results so no patient was left with a stranded result. Added monitoring so future silent drops surface as an alert within the lab instead of surfacing as a clinician complaint days later. Clinician-reported interface incidents fell to near zero for that failure mode.

**Possible limitations:** Interface troubleshooting is often reactive — you're chasing a problem that already reached patients, so the real fix is the monitoring that catches the *next* one proactively, and standing that up is the harder, less glamorous half. Root causes can sit on the vendor side, where your control is limited to escalation. Reconciling historical affected results is labor-intensive and depends on being able to identify them reliably. And fixing one message-content failure mode doesn't immunize the interface against others — the durable lesson is monitoring and message validation, not the single fix.

**30-second description:** "Clinicians reported results intermittently not showing up in the EHR — the dangerous, silent kind of failure. I worked the problem systematically: established the pattern, pulled the interface-engine logs to find where in the LIS-to-EHR chain messages were dropping, and inspected the actual HL7 content, which revealed a message-content condition the EHR was silently rejecting. We fixed the handling, reconciled the historical affected results so no patient was stranded, and added monitoring so the next silent drop surfaces as an alert instead of a complaint."

**2-minute explanation:** "An interface is the pipe between the lab and the clinician, and a leaky pipe is a direct safety hazard — a result that never reaches the EHR is, from the bedside, a result that doesn't exist. This was the worst kind of leak: intermittent. It wasn't a full outage that everyone notices and everyone fixes; it was a slow, silent drop of a fraction of results, which erodes trust and, in the worst case, means a clinician misses a critical value entirely. And the root cause could have been anywhere along the chain — the LIS, the interface engine, the HL7 message itself, or the EHR. So I treated it as a systematic trace, not a guess. First, establish the pattern — which analytes, which locations, which times — because intermittent problems almost always have a signature that points you toward the cause. Then pull the interface-engine transaction logs to localize *where* messages were being lost: did the LIS send the result message, did the engine receive and route it, did the EHR acknowledge it? That's what let us stop guessing. When we inspected the actual HL7 content of the failing cases, we found the tell — certain results carried a message-content condition, an unexpected special character and an overlong comment field, that the EHR interface was silently rejecting without surfacing an obvious error back to the lab. We worked with the interface team and the vendor to fix the message handling, we reconciled the historical affected results so no patient was left with a stranded result — that closure step is a safety obligation, not optional — and, crucially, we added monitoring so a future silent drop shows up as an alert inside the lab instead of as a clinician complaint days later. Reported interface incidents for that failure mode dropped to nearly zero. The honest framing: interface troubleshooting is often reactive, so the real durable win isn't the one fix — it's the monitoring and message validation that catch the *next* problem before it reaches a patient."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "The instinct that matters is to trace it systematically along the LIS-to-engine-to-HL7-to-EHR chain instead of guessing, and to close the loop by reconciling the results that already dropped. But the real lesson is monitoring — an intermittent silent interface failure should surface as an alert in the lab, never as a clinician complaint."

**Competency demonstrated:** HL7 interface architecture fluency; systematic root-cause diagnosis across a multi-component chain; reading message logs and HL7 content; patient-safety closure (reconciling stranded results); the shift from reactive fix to proactive monitoring; recognizing that interface complexity grows with system integration.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If the interface analyst did the technical trace: "I wasn't reading the HL7 logs myself, but I was the physician who framed it as a safety problem, insisted we reconcile the historical affected results, and pushed for monitoring so it couldn't recur silently — the clinical urgency and the closure discipline were mine." If your experience is conceptual, show you understand the *reasoning*: "I haven't personally traced an interface failure, but I understand how to approach it — establish the pattern, use the interface-engine logs to localize where in the chain it's failing, inspect the actual HL7 content, close the loop on affected results, and add monitoring. And I understand that in a multi-site system, interfaces multiply, so this fluency only gets more important."
