# Project Bank (Part 2)

> **How to use this section.** These ten projects (11–20) are a *story library*, not a résumé. Each one is a fully worked, realistic laboratory improvement project that maps onto a competency a Lahey/BILH clinical pathologist interview is likely to probe: blood bank workflow, coagulation and hematology optimization, POCT oversight, molecular/flow integration, downtime resilience, critical-value handling, reporting standardization, committee work, and change management. Use them three ways: (1) as **models** for how a good CP improvement story is structured — problem, stakeholders, intervention, honest results; (2) as **raw material** you can adapt to your own experience using the *"How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it"* field on every card; and (3) as **spoken answers** — each card gives you a 30-second version, a 2-minute version, and interview-friendly phrasing you can say almost verbatim.
>
> **A word on honesty.** The metrics here are deliberately realistic, not heroic. Real lab projects move turnaround time by minutes and error rates by fractions of a percent, and they come with limitations. Interviewers at this level can smell an inflated story instantly. The most persuasive thing you can do is describe a modest, well-reasoned improvement, own its limits, and show you understood *why* it worked. Never claim you led something you supported — the adaptation field exists precisely so you can tell these stories truthfully from a contributor's seat.
>
> **On specificity.** Where a project references a vendor, analyzer family, or standard (HL7, LOINC, SNOMED CT, UCUM, AABB, CLSI, CAP, CLIA), it is used illustratively to show fluency, not as a claim about any specific institution's systems. Swap in the names of the systems you actually worked with.

## How each project card is organized

Every card below uses the same fields, in the same order:

- **Project title** — a plain, descriptive name.
- **Setting** — the kind of lab and scale (kept generic and adaptable).
- **Problem** — the operational or quality pain point, stated concretely.
- **Why it mattered** — the patient-care, quality, or operational stake.
- **Stakeholders** — who had to be in the room.
- **Intervention** — what was actually done, step by step.
- **Metrics** — what was measured (baseline → target logic).
- **Results** — honest, realistic outcomes.
- **Possible limitations** — where the project fell short or where the data is soft.
- **30-second description** — an elevator version.
- **2-minute explanation** — the full spoken story.
- **Interview-friendly phrasing** — sentences you can say nearly verbatim.
- **Competency demonstrated** — what the story proves about you.
- **How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it** — how to tell it truthfully from a supporting role.

## Project 11 — Blood Bank Workflow Improvement: Reducing Wrong-Blood-in-Tube Risk and Emergency-Release Friction

**Project title:** Closing the pre-analytic gap in transfusion — electronic patient identification at the draw and a cleaner emergency-release pathway.

**Setting:** A community teaching hospital transfusion service supporting a busy ED, an OR block, and an oncology infusion center; roughly 12,000–15,000 RBC units transfused per year, with a mix of type-and-screen and full crossmatch workflows.

**Problem:** Two linked problems. First, a low but non-zero rate of **wrong-blood-in-tube (WBIT)** events — specimens whose labeled identity did not match the patient — surfaced through the historical ABO discrepancy check (a second, independently drawn confirmatory type before electronic issue). Each near-miss triggered a redraw, delayed the crossmatch, and represented a real patient-harm potential if it had slipped through. Second, the **emergency-release ("uncrossmatched O-negative") process** was paper-driven and inconsistently documented, which created both clinical delay during massive hemorrhage and a documentation gap that surfaced at CAP inspection.

**Why it mattered:** WBIT is one of the few laboratory errors that can directly kill a patient through an acute hemolytic transfusion reaction. The transfusion chain is a classic "systems" problem — the fatal defect is almost always introduced at the bedside during collection, far upstream of the blood bank, yet the blood bank owns the consequence. Emergency release, meanwhile, is exactly the situation where friction costs the most: a bleeding trauma patient cannot wait on a fax and a wet signature.

**Stakeholders:** The transfusion medical director (accountable owner), the blood bank supervisor and lead technologists, nursing leadership from the ED and OR (because the fix lives at the bedside), the LIS/informatics team, the phlebotomy/patient-access group, patient safety/quality, and the trauma service for the emergency-release piece.

**Intervention:**
1. **Pre-analytic identity hardening.** Advocated for and helped implement barcode-based positive patient identification at the point of collection for type-and-screen specimens — scanning the patient wristband and the specimen label at the bedside rather than relying on hand-labeling. Where a full electronic solution was not yet feasible for every draw, reinforced the **two-sample rule** (a second independently collected ABO confirmation before electronic crossmatch/issue for first-time patients).
2. **Rule logic in the LIS/BBIS.** Configured the blood bank information system so that electronic issue is *blocked* until a valid historical or second-draw ABO type is on file, making the safeguard a hard stop rather than a policy people could shortcut under pressure.
3. **Emergency-release redesign.** Replaced the paper emergency-release process with a structured electronic order and standardized packet, with the physician attestation and the "uncrossmatched, life-threatening indication" documentation captured in a way that satisfies AABB/CAP requirements *after* the units are already moving — decoupling the paperwork from the clinical action.

**Metrics:**
- WBIT / ABO-discrepancy near-miss rate (events per 10,000 type-and-screen specimens).
- Time from emergency-release order to units leaving the blood bank.
- Documentation completeness rate for emergency releases (audit sample).
- Redraw rate on transfusion specimens.

**Results:** ABO-discrepancy near-misses fell meaningfully after the second-sample hard stop and bedside scanning were in place — from a handful of catches per year to near-zero *detected discrepancies reaching the confirmation step*, with the interpretation that the defect was being intercepted at the bedside instead. Emergency-release documentation completeness on audit went from inconsistent (roughly two-thirds complete) to consistently near-complete, and the process was clean at the next CAP inspection. Emergency-release *speed* improved modestly — the paperwork stopped being on the critical path — though the dominant time driver (getting O-negative units physically to the patient) was already fast.

**Possible limitations:** The near-miss count is small, so the "improvement" is partly a story about a rare event — hard to prove statistically. Bedside scanning depends on nursing/phlebotomy adherence, which drifts without reinforcement; sustainment required ongoing audit. And the biggest residual risk — a WBIT that is correctly typed by luck (patient and mislabeled specimen share a blood type) — is invisible to ABO confirmation and can only be addressed by the bedside identity step, so the intervention reduces but never eliminates the hazard.

**30-second description:** "We tightened the pre-analytic end of transfusion — the part that actually kills people. We made a valid second ABO type a hard stop in the blood bank system before any electronic issue, pushed barcode patient ID to the bedside draw, and rebuilt the emergency-release process so the paperwork no longer sat on the critical path during a hemorrhage. Near-misses dropped and our emergency-release documentation went from spotty to inspection-clean."

**2-minute explanation:** "The transfusion service had two related issues. We were catching a small number of wrong-blood-in-tube events through our confirmatory ABO check — good that we caught them, but each one meant the real defect had been introduced upstream at the bedside, and a miss could be fatal. Separately, our emergency-release process was paper-based, which was both slow at the wrong moment and left documentation gaps that showed up at inspection. I worked with the transfusion director, the blood bank supervisor, nursing, and informatics. On the safety side, we made the fix structural rather than procedural: we configured the blood bank system so electronic issue is physically blocked until there's a valid historical or independently drawn second ABO type on file — you can't shortcut it under pressure — and we pushed barcode patient identification to the point of collection so the identity is verified where the error actually happens. On the emergency side, we replaced the paper packet with a structured electronic order that captures the physician attestation and indication in a way that meets AABB and CAP requirements, but *after* the units are already going out, so the documentation stops competing with the clinical action. Results were honest: our detected ABO discrepancies dropped toward zero, emergency-release documentation completeness went from about two-thirds to near-complete and was clean at the next CAP inspection, and the process got a little faster mainly because paperwork left the critical path. The limitation I'm candid about is that a wrong specimen that happens to share the patient's blood type is invisible to an ABO check — the only real defense there is the bedside identity step, which depends on adherence, so we built in ongoing audit."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "The lesson I took from that project is that in transfusion, the fatal error is almost always born at the bedside, upstream of the blood bank — so the highest-leverage fix is a hard stop in the system plus positive patient ID at the draw, not more diligence from the techs who are already downstream of the problem."

**Competency demonstrated:** Transfusion safety judgment, pre-analytic systems thinking, using LIS/BBIS rule logic to enforce policy structurally, regulatory (AABB/CAP) documentation fluency, and cross-departmental collaboration with nursing.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you were a resident, fellow, or covering pathologist rather than the transfusion director, frame it as: "I was part of the transfusion service when we worked through this. My contribution was [reviewing the ABO discrepancies as they came up / helping specify the hard-stop rule logic with informatics / auditing emergency-release documentation]. What I took from it is the systems reasoning..." You keep the insight and the specific mechanism while being precise about your role. Even "I reviewed every WBIT near-miss for root cause" is a strong, true contributor story.

## Project 12 — Coagulation Testing Optimization: Fixing Sample-Quality Rejections and Reflex Logic

**Project title:** Reducing coagulation specimen rejections and building smarter reflex testing for prolonged clotting times.

**Setting:** A core laboratory coagulation bench running routine PT/INR, aPTT, fibrinogen, D-dimer, and a reflex menu (mixing studies, factor assays, thrombin time) on an automated coag analyzer, supporting inpatient anticoagulation management, a busy ED, and an anticoagulation clinic.

**Problem:** Coagulation is unusually sensitive to pre-analytic quality — underfilled citrate tubes, hemolysis, clotted specimens, and delayed processing all corrupt results. The bench had a **high specimen rejection and redraw rate**, concentrated in short-filled blue-top tubes and clotted samples, driving repeated venipunctures and delayed anticoagulation decisions. Separately, when a PT or aPTT came back prolonged, the workup was **inconsistent and clinician-driven** — some got appropriate mixing studies and factor assays, others bounced back and forth with add-on orders, lengthening the time to a usable answer for the clinical team.

**Why it mattered:** A delayed or wrong coagulation result directly affects anticoagulation dosing, bleeding workups, and pre-procedure clearance. Every redraw delays a heparin or warfarin decision and re-sticks the patient. And an unstructured prolonged-clotting-time workup means a bleeding or thrombosis patient waits longer for a diagnosis while the lab does testing in a slow, reactive sequence.

**Why it mattered (patient framing):** For a pre-op patient on the OR schedule, a rejected coag specimen at 6 a.m. can push a case; for a bleeding inpatient, an unstructured factor workup can add a day.

**Stakeholders:** The coagulation section director/CP, the core lab supervisor, lead coag technologists, phlebotomy leadership (the rejection problem is largely a collection problem), the LIS/informatics team for reflex-rule build, and hematology/anticoagulation service physicians as the primary consumers.

**Intervention:**
1. **Attacked the pre-analytic root cause.** Analyzed rejections by cause and collection location, which showed short-fill and clotting clustered on specific draw sites and specific tube-handling steps. Standardized tube fill (citrate ratio depends on the 9:1 fill), reinforced correct order-of-draw and inversion, and worked with phlebotomy on targeted retraining at the highest-rejection locations.
2. **Middleware-based quality flags.** Used analyzer/middleware checks (e.g., automatic detection of interfering hemolysis/lipemia and out-of-range fill) to catch bad specimens earlier and route them consistently rather than variably.
3. **Structured reflex logic.** Built a defined, LIS-driven reflex pathway: a prolonged aPTT with a specified pattern reflexes to a mixing study; a non-correcting mixing study routes toward inhibitor/lupus-anticoagulant workup while a correcting one routes toward factor assays — with the CP retaining oversight of the interpretive step. This replaced the piecemeal add-on ordering with a coherent, faster sequence.
4. **Interpretive comments.** Standardized result comments so clinicians got consistent guidance (e.g., how to interpret a corrected vs. non-corrected mix) rather than a bare number.

**Metrics:**
- Coagulation specimen rejection/redraw rate (% of coag specimens).
- Turnaround time for a completed prolonged-clotting-time workup (order to final interpretation).
- Proportion of prolonged results that followed the standardized reflex pathway vs. ad-hoc add-ons.
- Clinician-add-on order volume for coag follow-up tests.

**Results:** The rejection rate dropped after the phlebotomy-targeted fixes — a realistic move from the high-single-digits/low-double-digits percent range typical of coag down toward the lower single digits at the worst sites, which is where most of the redraws lived. The structured reflex logic cut the time to a complete prolonged-clotting workup by consolidating what used to be several rounds of clinician add-ons into a single defined sequence, and it reduced back-and-forth phone calls between the lab and the wards. The improvement was uneven — the biggest gains were at the specific collection sites and in the specific reflex patterns we targeted, not a uniform system-wide leap.

**Possible limitations:** Pre-analytic gains are adherence-dependent and tend to decay after the retraining energy fades, so sustainment needs a recurring rejection dashboard. The reflex logic can over-test if the trigger criteria are too loose — a mixing study on every mildly prolonged aPTT wastes reagent and can generate noise — so the criteria required careful tuning and CP oversight to avoid trading a delay problem for an over-utilization problem. And turnaround-time gains on the workup depend on assay batching and reagent availability, which the reflex logic alone doesn't fix.

**30-second description:** "Coagulation was bleeding time and money on two fronts — too many rejected, short-filled, or clotted specimens driving redraws, and a chaotic, clinician-driven workup for prolonged clotting times. We went after the pre-analytic root cause with targeted phlebotomy fixes and middleware quality flags, and we built a defined LIS reflex pathway so a prolonged aPTT moves cleanly through mixing study to factor assays. Redraws dropped and the workup got faster and more consistent."

**2-minute explanation:** "Coag is the most pre-analytically fragile bench in the core lab — the citrate fill ratio, hemolysis, clotting, timing all matter — and we had two problems. Our specimen rejection rate was high, concentrated in short-filled and clotted tubes, which meant redraws and delayed anticoagulation decisions. And when a PT or aPTT came back prolonged, the follow-up was ad hoc: clinicians added on tests one at a time, so a bleeding-disorder workup took several rounds. I worked with the coag director, the core lab supervisor, phlebotomy, and informatics. First, I analyzed the rejections by cause and location — the short-fills and clots clustered at a few draw sites, so we did targeted retraining on fill and order-of-draw there rather than a blanket lecture, and we turned on middleware flags for hemolysis and fill so bad specimens were caught consistently. Second, we replaced the piecemeal follow-up with a structured reflex pathway in the LIS: a prolonged aPTT with the right pattern reflexes automatically to a mixing study, and the mix result routes the workup — non-correcting toward an inhibitor or lupus-anticoagulant path, correcting toward factor assays — with me keeping oversight of the interpretive call, plus standardized comments so clinicians got real guidance, not just a number. Honestly, the rejection rate came down mostly at the sites we targeted, and the workup turnaround improved by collapsing several add-on rounds into one sequence. The limitation I'm upfront about is that reflex logic can over-test if the triggers are too loose, so we had to tune the criteria carefully to avoid trading a delay problem for an over-utilization one, and the pre-analytic gains need a standing dashboard or they drift back."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "What I like about that project is that it's two different kinds of informatics problem in one bench — a pre-analytic quality problem you solve with data-driven targeting and middleware, and a workflow problem you solve with disciplined reflex logic. And both had to be tuned carefully so we didn't over-test."

**Competency demonstrated:** Pre-analytic quality analysis, phlebotomy collaboration, middleware/instrument-flag fluency, reflex-rule design with utilization discipline, and interpretive-comment standardization.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you supported the coag bench without owning it, lead with the analytic piece you did: "I did the root-cause analysis on our coag rejections and found they clustered at specific draw sites" or "I helped design the reflex logic for prolonged aPTTs and made sure the mixing-study interpretation stayed under pathologist oversight." If your involvement was purely interpretive, you can still tell the story as: "I signed out the prolonged-coag workups and pushed for a more structured reflex pathway because I kept seeing the same slow, piecemeal ordering."

## Project 13 — Hematology Workflow Streamlining: Autoverification and Smart Slide-Review Rules

**Project title:** Expanding CBC autoverification and rationalizing manual differential/smear-review criteria on the hematology line.

**Setting:** A high-volume automated hematology section running CBC with automated differential on a modern analyzer line (with reflex slide-maker/stainer and digital imaging), producing several thousand CBCs a day for inpatient, ED, and outpatient populations.

**Problem:** The hematology line had **low autoverification rates** and **overly broad manual-review criteria** — too many normal or clinically insignificant CBCs were being held for technologist review or reflexed to a manual smear, creating a review backlog, inflating turnaround time, and consuming skilled tech time on samples that didn't need it. At the same time, the flagging rules were not consistently catching the results that *did* warrant a smear (blasts, schistocytes, significant left shift), so the section was simultaneously over-reviewing and occasionally under-reviewing.

**Why it mattered:** A CBC held unnecessarily delays an ED disposition or an inpatient decision; a smear that should have been reviewed but wasn't can miss a first presentation of leukemia or a microangiopathic process. Technologist time spent scanning clearly normal smears is time not spent on the samples that actually need a trained eye — a quality and a staffing problem at once. In a lab with a thin overnight bench, review backlog is where turnaround time quietly dies.

**Stakeholders:** The hematology CP/section director, the core lab supervisor and hematology lead techs, the LIS/middleware team, and the ED and hospitalist services as the main turnaround-time consumers. Manufacturer application specialists for the analyzer's flagging logic were consulted.

**Intervention:**
1. **Baseline the review funnel.** Measured what fraction of CBCs autoverified, what fraction were held for tech review, and what fraction reflexed to manual smear — and *why*. This exposed a set of flag rules that were triggering review on clinically trivial patterns.
2. **Rebuilt autoverification rules against consensus criteria.** Reworked the middleware autoverification and slide-review rules using established frameworks (the International Consensus Group for Hematology slide-review criteria as a reference point), tightening triggers so that normal and stable results release automatically while genuinely concerning flags (immature-cell flags, extreme values, significant deltas, morphology alarms) are consistently held.
3. **Delta checks and previous-result logic.** Added delta-check and prior-smear logic so that a stable chronic abnormality (e.g., a longstanding mild anemia) doesn't get re-flagged every draw, while a new or rapidly changing abnormality does.
4. **Digital-imaging triage.** Where digital cell imaging was available, used the pre-classified images to let techs triage smears faster and to standardize what a "review" actually looks like.
5. **Validated before go-live.** Ran the new rule set in parallel against manual review to confirm it wasn't releasing anything it shouldn't, documented the validation, and got CP sign-off — because autoverification rules are a patient-safety control and must be validated and inspection-defensible.

**Metrics:**
- Autoverification rate (% of CBCs released without human touch).
- Manual smear-review rate (% reflexed to smear).
- CBC turnaround time (result-to-verification), especially for the ED.
- False-negative check: rate of clinically significant findings that the new rules would have released without review (measured during validation).

**Results:** Autoverification rose substantially — a realistic move from a middling rate into the higher range appropriate for the population (well-run hematology lines often autoverify a large majority of routine CBCs), which pulled routine CBC turnaround time down and cleared the review backlog. The manual smear-review rate dropped because trivial triggers were removed, freeing tech time for the smears that mattered. Crucially, the validation showed the *tighter-but-smarter* rules did not increase missed significant findings — the point was not just "review less," but "review the right ones." Gains were most visible during high-volume periods and thin-staffing shifts, where the backlog had been worst.

**Possible limitations:** Autoverification rules are a permanent maintenance commitment — analyzer software updates, reagent/lot changes, and population shifts can silently change flag behavior, so the rules need periodic re-review and re-validation. There is an inherent, uncomfortable trade-off: every increase in autoverification slightly widens the theoretical window for an unreviewed abnormal to slip through, which is why the validation and the ongoing monitoring are non-negotiable rather than optional. And the digital-imaging benefit depends on the quality of the analyzer's pre-classification, which is good but not perfect and still requires tech judgment.

**30-second description:** "Our hematology line was over-reviewing — too many normal CBCs held for tech review or reflexed to a smear — which choked turnaround time and burned tech time, while occasionally missing the flags that mattered. We rebuilt the autoverification and slide-review rules against consensus criteria, added delta and prior-result logic, validated it in parallel before go-live, and pushed autoverification up sharply without increasing missed significant findings."

**2-minute explanation:** "The hematology bench had a paradox: it was simultaneously over-reviewing and under-reviewing. A lot of clearly normal CBCs were being held for technologist review or reflexed to manual smears on trivial triggers, which created a review backlog and slowed turnaround — but the same rule set wasn't reliably catching the things that actually need a human, like blasts or schistocytes. I worked with the heme director, the lead techs, and the middleware team. We started by measuring the review funnel — what autoverified, what was held, what reflexed, and why — and it was obvious a handful of flag rules were firing on clinically meaningless patterns. We rebuilt the autoverification and slide-review rules using the international consensus slide-review criteria as our reference, tightened the triggers so stable and normal results release automatically, and added delta checks and prior-result logic so a longstanding chronic abnormality doesn't get re-flagged every single draw while a new or fast-changing one does. Where we had digital cell imaging, we used the pre-classified images to make review faster and more standardized. The part I insisted on was validating the new rules in parallel against manual review before go-live, documenting it, and signing off — because autoverification is a patient-safety control, not just an efficiency tweak. The result was a big jump in autoverification and a drop in unnecessary smear reviews, which pulled turnaround down and freed tech time for the smears that mattered — and critically, the validation showed we weren't releasing significant findings that should have been reviewed. My honest caveat is that these rules are a standing maintenance commitment: analyzer updates and lot changes can shift flag behavior, so they need periodic re-validation."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "The framing I use for autoverification is that it's not about reviewing *less* — it's about reviewing the *right* results. Done well, you increase both efficiency and safety at once, but only if you validate it and treat the rules as a living control that needs re-checking, not a set-and-forget config."

**Competency demonstrated:** Autoverification design and validation, consensus-criteria fluency (ICSH/consensus slide-review rules), middleware and delta-check logic, patient-safety-conscious efficiency, and CP oversight of a rule-based control.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you weren't the heme director, anchor to the validation or analysis piece: "I helped validate our expanded autoverification rules against manual review before go-live" or "I did the analysis of our slide-review funnel that showed we were reflexing too many trivial CBCs." If you were more junior, the honest and impressive version is: "I reviewed the flagged smears and kept noticing we were pulling normal ones and occasionally missing real morphology, which is what prompted us to re-examine the rules."

## Project 14 — POCT Oversight Example: Connecting and Standardizing Point-of-Care Glucose and Blood Gas

**Project title:** Bringing decentralized point-of-care testing under real oversight — connectivity, operator competency, and QC compliance across nursing units.

**Setting:** A hospital-wide point-of-care testing program spanning dozens of nursing units, the ED, ICUs, and OR — hundreds of glucometers plus point-of-care blood gas and iSTAT-type devices, operated by hundreds of nurses and respiratory therapists rather than laboratory staff.

**Problem:** POCT is the part of the lab that isn't in the lab. Devices lived on the units, were operated by non-lab staff, and the program had **inconsistent QC documentation, lapsed operator competencies, and weak connectivity** — some devices weren't reliably transmitting results into the LIS/EHR, meaning results were being acted on clinically but not always captured in the permanent record or subject to the lab's QC discipline. This is a classic CAP/CLIA vulnerability: POCT is held to the same regulatory standard as the main lab, but it's the hardest to control because the lab doesn't own the operators.

**Why it mattered:** A point-of-care glucose drives insulin dosing in real time; a point-of-care blood gas drives ventilator and resuscitation decisions. If the device is out of calibration, the operator isn't competency-verified, or QC is being skipped, patients are being treated on unvalidated numbers — and the lab is accountable at inspection whether or not it feels in control. Poor connectivity also means clinicians sometimes re-order a central lab test because they can't find the POC result, wasting a draw and time.

**Stakeholders:** The POCT medical director / CP, the POCT coordinator and lab QC staff, nursing education and unit managers (who control the operators), respiratory therapy leadership, information systems (for device connectivity and interface build), and biomedical engineering. This is the most **cross-departmental** of all the projects because the lab has responsibility without direct authority over the operators.

**Intervention:**
1. **Inventory and connectivity.** Built an accurate inventory of every POC device and its connectivity status, then prioritized getting the disconnected/poorly connected devices onto the data-management system so results flow to the LIS/EHR and QC is captured centrally.
2. **Data-management middleware.** Leveraged a POCT data-management system (e.g., a connectivity platform following the POCT1-A standard) so that device QC, operator lockouts, and results are managed from one place instead of device-by-device on the units.
3. **Operator competency as a hard control.** Moved operator competency from a paper roster to a system where an operator whose competency has lapsed is **locked out** of the device — turning competency from a documentation exercise into an enforced control.
4. **QC lockout and scheduling.** Configured devices to lock out if scheduled QC isn't performed and passing, so a unit can't keep testing on a device that hasn't had its required QC.
5. **Standardization across units.** Harmonized QC frequency, critical-value handling, and correlation-with-central-lab procedures so that a glucose in the ICU is governed the same way as one in a med-surg unit — important because as a system integrates, POCT variability between sites is a real risk.

**Metrics:**
- Device connectivity rate (% of POC results captured electronically in LIS/EHR).
- QC compliance rate (% of required QC performed and passing on schedule).
- Operator competency currency (% of active operators with current competency).
- POCT-related citations/findings at CAP inspection.

**Results:** Connectivity improved markedly as previously stranded devices were brought onto the data-management platform, so far more POC results were captured in the permanent record and available to clinicians without a re-draw. QC compliance and operator-competency currency rose substantially once lockouts made them enforced rather than requested — the lockout is the whole point: it converts a "please do your QC" culture into a "you can't test without it" control. The program went into its next CAP inspection materially cleaner on POCT. Realistically, the biggest early wins were on the units that had been the worst offenders.

**Possible limitations:** POCT oversight is never "done" — new devices, new units, and staff turnover constantly erode currency, so it's a permanent program, not a project with an end. Lockouts create real clinical friction if mis-tuned (you do not want a legitimately needed glucose blocked at 3 a.m. because of a QC-scheduling quirk), so the enforcement has to be designed with clinical safety in mind and with clear override/escalation paths. And the lab's fundamental structural problem persists: it is accountable for operators it doesn't manage, so sustainment depends entirely on the strength of the partnership with nursing and respiratory therapy.

**30-second description:** "POCT is the lab that isn't in the lab — hundreds of devices run by nurses and RTs, held to full CLIA standards but hard to control. We built an accurate device inventory, got the disconnected ones onto a data-management platform so results actually reach the record, and turned operator competency and QC into enforced device lockouts instead of paper rosters. Connectivity, QC compliance, and competency currency all jumped, and we went into CAP much cleaner."

**2-minute explanation:** "Point-of-care testing is the hardest part of the lab to govern because it physically isn't in the lab — it's hundreds of glucometers and blood-gas devices out on the units, operated by nurses and respiratory therapists, but it's held to exactly the same CLIA and CAP standard as our main analyzers. We had inconsistent QC documentation, lapsed operator competencies, and weak connectivity — some devices weren't reliably sending results into the LIS, so clinicians were dosing insulin and adjusting ventilators on numbers that weren't always in the permanent record or under our QC discipline. This was the most cross-departmental project I've been part of, because the lab has the responsibility but nursing and RT own the operators. We started by building an honest inventory of every device and its connectivity status, then prioritized getting the stranded ones onto a POCT data-management platform so QC, operator management, and results run through one system. The key move was making the controls *enforced*: an operator whose competency has lapsed gets locked out of the device, and a device that hasn't had its scheduled QC locks out — so competency and QC stop being things we ask for and become things you can't test without. We also standardized QC frequency and critical-value handling across units so a glucose in the ICU is governed the same as one on a med-surg floor. Connectivity, QC compliance, and competency currency all improved substantially, and we went into the next CAP inspection much cleaner. My honest caveats are two: this is a permanent program because turnover constantly erodes it, and lockouts have to be tuned carefully — you never want to block a genuinely needed test at 3 a.m. over a scheduling quirk, so we built in override and escalation paths."

**Interview-friendly phrasing:** "The thing I understand about POCT is that the lab has full regulatory accountability but no direct authority over the operators — so the winning strategy is to move controls into the device itself, through connectivity and lockouts, and to invest heavily in the nursing and RT partnership, because you can't audit your way out of a relationship problem."

**Competency demonstrated:** POCT regulatory grounding (CLIA/CAP), connectivity and data-management (POCT1-A) fluency, enforced-control design, cross-departmental leadership without direct authority, and standardization across sites — directly relevant to multi-site integration.

**How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you supported rather than directed the POCT program, lead with a concrete slice: "I helped bring our disconnected glucometers onto the data-management system" or "I worked on standardizing POCT critical-value handling across units." If your role was mostly oversight review, the honest version is: "As part of POCT oversight I reviewed the QC and competency compliance and pushed for the lockout controls when I saw how much was slipping through on paper rosters."

## Project 15 — Molecular / Flow Cytometry Integration Example: End-to-End Result Reporting for a New Molecular Assay

**Project title:** Integrating a new molecular infectious-disease (or oncology) assay end-to-end — from instrument to LIS to EHR with correct coding and interpretive reporting — and, in parallel, cleaning up flow cytometry result integration.

**Setting:** A molecular diagnostics section standing up a new PCR-based assay (illustratively, a respiratory viral panel or a quantitative viral load / oncology mutation assay), plus a flow cytometry service reporting immunophenotyping for hematologic malignancy — both feeding into the same LIS and EHR.

**Problem:** New molecular and flow assays are informatics-heavy at exactly the point where labs tend to under-invest: **result integration**. The instrument produces a result, but getting it correctly and safely into the patient record involves test build, result mapping, coding (LOINC for the test/result, SNOMED CT for organisms/interpretations, correct units via UCUM for quantitative assays), interpretive-comment logic, and interface validation. The specific problems were: a new molecular assay whose results needed to cross from the instrument/middleware through the LIS to the EHR **without truncation, unit errors, or mismapping**, and a flow cytometry service whose complex interpretive reports were being entered/transmitted inconsistently, so the nuanced immunophenotyping interpretation didn't always render cleanly for the ordering oncologist.

**Why it mattered:** A molecular viral load that crosses the interface with the wrong units or a dropped decimal is a direct patient-safety event — it changes clinical decisions in HIV, hepatitis, or transplant monitoring. A qualitative pathogen result that maps to the wrong SNOMED concept can mislead infection control and public-health reporting. And a flow cytometry interpretation that renders as a garbled block of text — or loses its interpretive comment — undermines the entire value of a subspecialty read; the oncologist needs the pathologist's synthesis, not just a list of positive and negative markers.

**Stakeholders:** The molecular/flow CP and section directors, the LIS/informatics build team, the interface analysts, the instrument/middleware vendor, infection prevention and public health (for reportable molecular results), and the ordering clinicians (ID, oncology, transplant) as consumers. For the coding, informatics standards expertise (LOINC/SNOMED/UCUM) was central.

**Intervention:**
1. **Mapped the full result path before go-live.** Traced the assay end-to-end: instrument → middleware → LIS → EHR, identifying every point where a value is transformed, coded, or could be truncated.
2. **Standards-based coding.** Assigned correct LOINC codes to the test and result, SNOMED CT concepts to qualitative interpretations/organisms, and UCUM units to quantitative results — so the data is interoperable and correctly interpretable downstream, including for public-health reporting and cross-system exchange (which matters directly as BILH integrates sites).
3. **Interpretive-comment architecture.** Built structured interpretive comments and reflex logic so a positive result carries the appropriate clinical guidance, and — for flow — so the pathologist's synthesized interpretation transmits as a coherent, readable narrative rather than fragmenting.
4. **End-to-end validation of the interface.** Ran test cases through the whole chain — including edge cases (very high/low values, special characters, long comments) — and confirmed the result *renders correctly in the EHR as the clinician will actually see it*, not just that it left the instrument correctly. This "look at it in the EHR" step is the one most often skipped and the one that catches truncation and formatting failures.
5. **Reportable-result routing.** For reportable molecular results, confirmed the correct electronic pathway to infection prevention/public health.

**Metrics:**
- Interface/mapping error rate found during validation (defects caught pre-go-live).
- Result-rendering fidelity in the EHR (structured audit of how results actually display).
- Time from result finalization to availability in the EHR.
- Post-go-live result-integration incident rate (mismapped, truncated, or mis-united results reaching clinicians).

**Results:** The end-to-end validation caught several defects *before go-live* that would otherwise have reached patients — the highest-value outcome, because a truncation or unit error caught in validation is a non-event, while the same error in production is an incident report. Post-go-live, the assay integrated cleanly with a very low integration-incident rate, and the flow cytometry interpretive reports rendered coherently for oncologists. The quantitative molecular results carried correct UCUM units and LOINC codes, which also made them cleanly consumable when data was shared across the system.

**Possible limitations:** End-to-end interface validation is labor-intensive and easy to shortcut under go-live time pressure — the temptation is to validate that the instrument produces the right number and assume the rest of the chain is fine, which is exactly where errors hide. Coding accuracy (LOINC/SNOMED/UCUM) depends on human mapping judgment and can be wrong in subtle ways that only surface later during data aggregation or cross-system exchange. And interpretive-comment logic can become unwieldy over time as new scenarios accrue, needing periodic pruning.

## Project 16 — Downtime Planning and Recovery: A Real LIS/Interface Downtime Playbook

**Project title:** Building and testing a laboratory downtime plan — planned and unplanned LIS/EHR/interface outages — and cleaning up the post-downtime result-reconciliation gap.

**Setting:** A core laboratory dependent on an LIS, an EHR, and a web of HL7 interfaces and middleware, running 24/7 and supporting an ED, ICUs, and inpatient units where results cannot simply "wait."

**Problem:** The lab's downtime procedures were **thin, outdated, and untested**. There was no clear, rehearsed workflow for what happens when the LIS goes down, the EHR interface drops, or middleware fails — how results get produced, verified, and communicated on paper; how critical values still get called; and, critically, how results generated *during* downtime get **reconciled back into the record when systems recover**. The recovery/backload gap was the most dangerous part: results produced on paper during an outage risked being missed, duplicated, or delayed in getting into the permanent record afterward.

**Why it mattered:** Downtime is inevitable — planned maintenance, upgrades, and unplanned failures all happen. During an outage, an ED or ICU still needs a potassium, a troponin, a lactate, and a critical value still has to reach a clinician. A lab that hasn't rehearsed downtime discovers its gaps at 2 a.m. during a real outage, which is the worst possible time. And the post-downtime reconciliation gap is a genuine patient-safety hazard: a critical result generated on paper during the outage that never makes it into the chart is exactly the kind of thing that leads to a sentinel event.

**Stakeholders:** The CP/lab director, the lab operations manager and bench supervisors, the LIS/informatics and interface teams, IT/EHR downtime coordinators, nursing and the ED/ICU (who receive results during downtime), and hospital emergency-management/incident-command for large outages.

**Intervention:**
1. **Classified downtime types and impact.** Distinguished planned vs. unplanned, and partial (one interface down) vs. total (LIS down), because the response differs — a dropped EHR interface with a working LIS is a different problem from a dead LIS.
2. **Built a concrete, role-based playbook.** Wrote step-by-step downtime procedures specifying exactly who does what, how results get produced and verified without the LIS (manual worklists, instrument-direct results, downtime result forms), how critical values are still identified and called, and how paper results are logged so they can be reconciled later.
3. **Fixed the reconciliation/backload process.** Defined precisely how downtime-period results are entered into the LIS/EHR once systems recover — with a reconciliation checklist so nothing generated on paper is lost or double-entered, and with a specific check that any critical values called during downtime are captured in the permanent record.
4. **Downtime toolkit and read-only access.** Ensured a "downtime kit" of forms and current reference ranges, and where possible a read-only/business-continuity view of recent EHR data so clinicians and the lab aren't flying completely blind during an outage.
5. **Actually rehearsed it.** Ran a tabletop and, where feasible, a live drill during a planned downtime window — because a downtime plan that has never been exercised is a document, not a capability. The drill exposed gaps (unclear critical-value ownership, an outdated contact list) that were fixed before a real event.

**Metrics:**
- Downtime-drill completion and gap-closure (qualitative but concrete: gaps found → gaps fixed).
- Time to full result-reconciliation after systems recover.
- Critical-value capture during downtime (were all downtime critical values verified as reaching the chart post-recovery?).
- Lost/duplicated result rate after downtime events.

**Results:** The rehearsal was the highest-value output — it surfaced real gaps (a stale contact list, ambiguity about who owns critical-value callbacks when the LIS is down) that would otherwise have been discovered live during a real outage. After the playbook and drill, post-downtime reconciliation was faster and cleaner, and there was a defined, auditable process ensuring downtime critical values reached the chart. The lab handled its next planned upgrade downtime smoothly, and its documented, tested downtime plan stood up well at CAP inspection (which specifically expects a tested downtime procedure).

**Possible limitations:** A downtime plan decays — staff turn over, systems change, and the plan needs periodic re-drilling or it quietly becomes obsolete again. Drills can't perfectly simulate the stress and scale of a real prolonged outage. And there's an honest tension: robust downtime procedures add steps and slow throughput during the outage, which is the correct trade-off but is felt as friction by staff in the moment, so buy-in requires explaining *why* the reconciliation discipline matters.

We classified the outage types, wrote a concrete role-based playbook — how results get produced, verified, and critical-called without the LIS — and, most importantly, fixed the post-downtime reconciliation so nothing produced on paper gets lost when systems recover. Then we actually drilled it, which exposed real gaps we fixed before a live outage. The next planned downtime went smoothly and the plan held up at CAP."

## Project 17 — Critical Value Workflow Improvement: Closing the Read-Back and Documentation Loop

**Project title:** Rebuilding the critical-value notification workflow — reliable escalation, documented read-back, and closed-loop confirmation.

**Setting:** A core laboratory generating critical results across chemistry, hematology, coagulation, and microbiology (positive blood cultures) for a hospital with an ED, ICUs, inpatient units, and a growing outpatient/ambulatory population where reaching the responsible provider is harder.

**Problem:** The critical-value process was **unreliable and inconsistently documented**. Techs sometimes struggled to reach the responsible provider (especially for outpatient results), read-back — the required confirmation that the receiver correctly heard the value — was inconsistently performed and documented, and there was **no reliable escalation pathway** when the first contact couldn't be reached, so some critical values took too long to be communicated or had incomplete documentation of who was told, when, and whether they confirmed it.

**Why it mattered:** A critical value is, by definition, a result that may require immediate clinical action — a critical potassium, a critical glucose, a positive blood culture, a markedly prolonged INR. The entire clinical value of catching it in the lab is lost if it doesn't reach the right clinician quickly and get confirmed. Failure to communicate a critical result is one of the most cited laboratory safety failures and a National Patient Safety Goal; it's the kind of gap that turns a correctly-performed test into a sentinel event. Read-back matters because a value misheard over a noisy phone line ("point four" vs. "four") can be as dangerous as no call at all.

**Stakeholders:** The CP/lab director, the lab supervisors and the technologists making the calls, the LIS/informatics team, nursing and the medical staff (receivers), the ambulatory/outpatient clinics (the hardest to reach), and patient safety/quality (critical-value performance is a tracked safety metric).

**Intervention:**
1. **Reviewed and rationalized the critical-value list.** Confirmed the critical-value thresholds were clinically appropriate and not so broad that the volume of calls itself became noise — an over-inclusive list causes alarm fatigue and dilutes attention from the truly urgent.
2. **Defined a clear escalation ladder.** Specified exactly who gets called, and — if unreachable within a defined time — who is next (covering provider, charge nurse, attending, rapid-response pathway), so a critical value never stalls because one person didn't answer.
3. **Built documented read-back into the workflow.** Made read-back a required, prompted step in the LIS notification workflow — the tech records the receiver's name/role, the time, and confirms read-back — so it's structurally captured, not left to memory.
4. **Automated notification support.** Where available, used automated/assisted notification tools and the LIS to flag criticals, queue them, and track outstanding (uncommunicated) criticals so none silently ages out.
5. **Solved the outpatient reach problem.** Built a specific pathway for outpatient/ambulatory criticals (defined clinic contacts, after-hours coverage, and a fallback), because "we couldn't reach the ordering provider" is where outpatient criticals fail.
6. **Monitored performance.** Tracked critical-value notification time and documentation completeness as an ongoing quality metric with feedback to the bench.

**Metrics:**
- Median time from critical result verification to provider notification.
- Read-back documentation completeness (% of criticals with documented read-back).
- Rate of criticals requiring escalation (and time-to-escalation).
- Outstanding/unreached critical-value rate (criticals not communicated within target).

**Results:** Documentation completeness improved sharply once read-back was a prompted, structural step rather than a manual habit — this was the cleanest, most reliable gain. Notification time improved, especially for the previously-problematic outpatient results, once a defined escalation ladder and outpatient pathway existed. The rate of criticals that "fell through" — unreached or undocumented — dropped toward zero because the LIS now tracked outstanding criticals so they couldn't silently age out. The process was clean and defensible at CAP inspection, which scrutinizes critical-value handling closely.

**Possible limitations:** The intervention improves *communication reliability*, not the underlying difficulty of reaching busy or unavailable providers — the escalation ladder helps but can't manufacture a clinician who answers. Prompted read-back adds seconds to every call, which techs feel as friction on high-volume shifts (worth it, but real). And an over-inclusive critical list remains a latent risk for alarm fatigue, so the list itself needs periodic clinical review — fixing the *workflow* doesn't fix a badly-calibrated *threshold list*.

**30-second description:** "Our critical-value process was unreliable — hard to reach providers, inconsistent read-back, no escalation when the first call failed. We reviewed the critical list so it wasn't over-inclusive, built a clear escalation ladder, made documented read-back a prompted step in the LIS, added a specific outpatient-reach pathway, and tracked outstanding criticals so none silently aged out. Documentation completeness and notification reliability improved sharply, and the process was clean at CAP."

## Project 18 — Reporting Standardization: Harmonizing Result Names, Units, and Reference Ranges Across the Lab

**Project title:** Standardizing test names, units, reference intervals, and interpretive reporting so a result means the same thing everywhere it appears.

**Setting:** A laboratory (or a set of laboratories preparing to integrate) where the same analyte was reported under different test names, occasionally different units, and with reference ranges and interpretive comments that varied by instrument, section, or site — all flowing into a shared EHR.

**Problem:** The same clinical concept was represented inconsistently. A given analyte might appear under two different result names; units weren't always harmonized; reference intervals differed between instruments or sites for reasons that weren't always method-justified; and interpretive comments were free-text and variable. For clinicians looking at trended results across encounters — or across sites within a system — this created **confusion, trending errors, and a real safety risk** when the "same" test looked different depending on where it was drawn. Underneath it was a missing standards layer: tests weren't consistently mapped to LOINC, so the data wasn't reliably interoperable.

**Why it mattered:** A clinician trending a patient's results needs the values to be comparable. If a creatinine, a hemoglobin A1c, or a therapeutic drug level is reported under different names or units at different sites, a trend line becomes misleading — and misleading trends drive wrong decisions. As BILH grows and integrates laboratories, this stops being a cosmetic annoyance and becomes a **patient-safety and interoperability imperative**: you cannot safely merge two labs' data if the "same" test isn't actually the same. Standardized LOINC mapping is what lets results from different sites aggregate correctly.

**Stakeholders:** The CP/lab directors across sections (and across sites, for an integration context), the LIS/informatics and interface teams, the medical staff / clinical consumers (whose trending depends on this), quality, and — for reference-interval decisions — the section directors who own method validation. Informatics-standards expertise (LOINC, UCUM) was central.

**Intervention:**
1. **Built a master test inventory.** Catalogued every test, its name(s), units, reference intervals, method, and interpretive comments — the unglamorous but essential first step, because you can't standardize what you haven't inventoried.
2. **Harmonized names and units.** Standardized result names and units to consistent, clinically clear conventions, using UCUM for units, so the same analyte reads the same way everywhere.
3. **Mapped everything to LOINC.** Assigned correct LOINC codes so each result is a defined, interoperable concept — the foundation for aggregating results across instruments and sites.
4. **Rationalized reference intervals.** Reviewed reference ranges: where differences between instruments/sites were method-justified, kept and clearly labeled them; where they were arbitrary drift, harmonized them — with section-director and validation oversight, because reference intervals are a validated medical decision, not a formatting choice.
5. **Standardized interpretive comments.** Replaced variable free-text with consistent, controlled interpretive comments so clinicians get uniform guidance.
6. **Governed it going forward.** Established a light governance process (a test-build/harmonization review step) so new tests get added consistently and the standardization doesn't erode.

**Metrics:**
- % of tests mapped to LOINC.
- % of shared analytes with harmonized names/units.
- Number of duplicate/inconsistent result names eliminated.
- Reduction in clinician queries/complaints about inconsistent or confusing results.
- Readiness for cross-site data aggregation (qualitative milestone).

**Results:** The concrete wins were a high LOINC-mapping rate, elimination of a meaningful number of duplicate/inconsistent result names, and harmonized units and interpretive comments for shared analytes — which reduced clinician confusion and trending errors and cut the trickle of "why does this test look different than last time" queries. The reference-interval review appropriately *preserved* method-justified differences (labeled clearly) while removing arbitrary variation. The most strategic outcome was integration-readiness: the standardized, LOINC-mapped data was in a state that could be aggregated across sites without silently comparing unlike results.

**Possible limitations:** Standardization is genuinely hard and politically sensitive — section directors and sites have legitimate reasons for some differences (different methods truly do have different reference intervals), so the work requires judgment about what *should* be harmonized versus what *must* remain distinct; over-harmonizing is its own safety error. It's labor-intensive and low-glamour. And without ongoing governance it decays as new tests get added ad hoc, so the governance step is what makes it durable rather than a one-time cleanup.

## Project 19 — Interdisciplinary Committee Project: A Lab Utilization / Test-Formulary Initiative

**Project title:** Chairing (or supporting) a lab utilization committee to curb unnecessary and redundant testing while protecting appropriate use.

**Setting:** A hospital laboratory utilization / diagnostic stewardship committee spanning pathology/lab, clinical services (medicine, surgery, ID, oncology), pharmacy, informatics, and finance — convened to address inappropriate test ordering.

**Problem:** Test utilization had grown organically and inefficiently: **duplicate orders** (the same test ordered multiple times a day when the analyte doesn't change that fast), **outdated or low-value tests** still on the menu, **inappropriate send-out testing** (expensive esoteric tests ordered without clear indication), and a lack of ordering guardrails in the EHR. This drove unnecessary cost, unnecessary phlebotomy (contributing to hospital-acquired anemia), and result noise that added little clinical value. But utilization work is delicate — heavy-handed restriction can block genuinely needed tests and alienate clinicians.

**Why it mattered:** Every unnecessary test costs money, adds a needless blood draw (repeated draws contribute to iatrogenic anemia, especially in ICU patients), and can generate a borderline-abnormal result that triggers a workup cascade. But the flip side is real: over-restricting testing harms patients and clinician relationships. So this is fundamentally an interdisciplinary judgment and communication problem, not a unilateral lab decision — the committee structure exists precisely because the lab can't and shouldn't make these calls alone.

**Stakeholders:** The lab/CP (as chair or key member), physician champions from the major ordering services (essential — clinicians accept guardrails from peers far more than from the lab), pharmacy, informatics/EHR (to build the guardrails), nursing, finance/administration, and infection prevention (for microbiology/antimicrobial stewardship overlap).

**Intervention:**
1. **Data first, opinions second.** Pulled utilization data to identify the highest-volume, highest-cost, and most-duplicated tests — grounding the committee in evidence rather than anecdote, which is what keeps utilization work from becoming a turf fight.
2. **Targeted, defensible interventions.** Prioritized a short list of high-yield, low-controversy changes: duplicate-order alerts (a soft stop when the same test was ordered within a defined interval), retiring genuinely obsolete tests, and adding indication prompts or approval steps for a few expensive send-outs — rather than a sweeping restriction that would provoke backlash.
3. **Built the guardrails in the EHR/informatics layer.** Worked with informatics to implement duplicate-check logic, order-frequency limits where clinically justified, and clinical-decision-support prompts at the point of order — so the guardrails live in the workflow, not in a memo nobody reads.
4. **Engaged clinicians as partners.** Ran the changes through physician champions and the relevant services before implementation, framed as stewardship (right test, right patient, right time) rather than cost-cutting, and built in easy override paths so a clinician who genuinely needs a test isn't blocked.
5. **Measured and fed back.** Tracked the effect and reported it back, and committed to revisiting anything that caused clinical friction.

**Metrics:**
- Duplicate-order rate for targeted tests (before/after the soft stops).
- Volume/cost of targeted low-value and send-out tests.
- Phlebotomy volume (as a proxy for draw burden).
- Clinician satisfaction / complaint rate (to ensure guardrails weren't harming care).

**Results:** The targeted duplicate-order soft stops produced a real, measurable drop in redundant testing for the specific tests targeted, with associated cost and draw-burden savings — and, importantly, without a spike in clinician complaints, because the interventions were soft stops with override paths rather than hard blocks. Retiring obsolete tests cleaned up the menu. The send-out guardrails reduced a few high-cost, low-indication orders. The gains were deliberately concentrated on a short list of defensible targets rather than a broad crackdown — which is exactly why it succeeded where blunt restriction efforts often fail.

**Possible limitations:** Utilization gains erode over time as ordering habits reassert themselves and new providers arrive, so it needs ongoing monitoring and periodic re-engagement. Soft stops can be clicked through, so the effect is real but bounded — harder stops save more but risk blocking needed care and damaging clinician trust, a trade-off that has to be made carefully. And the savings, while real, are usually modest relative to the total lab budget; the honest value is often as much about reducing draw burden and result noise as about dollars. The whole effort lives or dies on clinician buy-in, which is relationship work that can't be shortcut.

**30-second description:** "We convened an interdisciplinary utilization committee to curb duplicate and low-value testing without blocking appropriate care. We led with data to pick a short list of defensible targets — duplicate soft-stops, retiring obsolete tests, indication prompts on a few expensive send-outs — built the guardrails into the EHR, and ran everything through physician champions framed as stewardship, not cost-cutting. Duplicate testing and draw burden dropped measurably, and we did it without a spike in clinician complaints."

**2-minute explanation:** "Utilization had grown organically into a mess: duplicate orders for tests that don't change that fast, obsolete tests still on the menu, expensive send-outs ordered without clear indication, and no ordering guardrails. Every unnecessary test is a cost, a needless blood draw that contributes to hospital-acquired anemia, and sometimes a borderline result that triggers an unnecessary workup. **How to adapt it if you did not directly lead it:** If you were a committee member rather than chair, that's completely legitimate to say: "I sat on our utilization committee and my contribution was the data analysis that identified our duplicate-testing targets" or "I helped design the duplicate-order soft-stop logic with informatics." ## Project 20 — Change Management Project: Rolling Out a Major LIS/Middleware Upgrade Without Breaking the Lab

**Project title:** Leading (or supporting) the human side of a major LIS or middleware upgrade — managing the transition so a technically sound change didn't fail operationally.

**Setting:** A core laboratory undergoing a significant system change — an LIS upgrade or migration, a new middleware/autoverification platform, or a new analyzer line integration — affecting daily workflows for dozens of technologists across all shifts, with the lab expected to run without interruption throughout.

**Problem:** The upgrade itself was well-scoped technically, but the real risk was **the human transition**. Major lab system changes routinely fail not because the technology is wrong but because the *change is managed poorly*: staff aren't adequately trained, workflows shift without clear communication, night and weekend shifts get less attention than days, resistance builds because people weren't consulted, and go-live becomes chaotic — with turnaround time and error rates spiking exactly when the lab can least afford it. The prior instinct in the lab had been to treat these as purely technical projects and under-invest in the people side.

**Why it mattered:** A lab has to keep running 24/7 through the transition — you can't pause patient care for an upgrade. If change is managed badly, the immediate cost is turnaround-time degradation, an error spike, and staff burnout/attrition during and after go-live; the longer-term cost is a workforce that resists the *next* change because the last one hurt. And in a system that's growing and integrating, the ability to manage change well is a recurring, strategic capability — integration *is* continuous change management. Doing it well once builds the trust and muscle for the next one.

**Stakeholders:** The CP/lab director and the operations manager (accountable owners), the bench supervisors and — critically — respected senior technologists on every shift (the informal opinion leaders whose buy-in the rest of the staff follow), the LIS/informatics and vendor teams, IT, and downstream consumers (nursing, clinicians) who need to know what's changing on their end.

**Intervention:**
1. **Treated it as change management, not just a technical cutover.** Applied a deliberate change framework — surfacing the reasons for the change, building a coalition of respected staff, communicating relentlessly, and planning for the transition and reinforcement, not just the go-live moment.
2. **Engaged the informal leaders early.** Identified respected senior techs on each shift and involved them in design and testing so they became advocates rather than skeptics — because in a lab, the night-shift lead's opinion moves more people than any memo from management.
3. **Communicated the "why," repeatedly and across all shifts.** Explained why the change was happening and what problem it solved, in plain terms, through multiple channels — and made a specific point of reaching nights and weekends, which are chronically under-communicated to and where go-live failures concentrate.
4. **Trained realistically and role-specifically.** Provided hands-on training in a test environment with role-specific, workflow-based scenarios (not just a vendor slide deck), and scheduled it so every shift was genuinely covered.
5. **Planned go-live and hypercare deliberately.** Chose a lower-volume go-live window, staffed up superusers on the floor during the transition (including nights), ran a validated downtime/fallback plan in case of trouble, and set up a rapid feedback loop so problems got fixed in hours, not days.
6. **Reinforced after go-live.** Kept superuser support and quick-reference aids in place through the stabilization period, gathered feedback, and closed the loop by fixing the pain points staff surfaced — which is what converts grudging compliance into genuine adoption.

**Metrics:**
- Turnaround-time stability through go-live (did TAT degrade, and how fast did it recover?).
- Error/incident rate during the transition period.
- Staff-readiness and training-completion rates by shift (including nights/weekends).
- Staff feedback / adoption sentiment and any transition-related attrition.

**Results:** The most meaningful outcome was that go-live was *uneventful* — turnaround time dipped only briefly and recovered quickly rather than spiking and staying elevated, and there was no significant error surge, because the staff were genuinely ready and superusers were on the floor across all shifts. The early engagement of respected senior techs converted potential resistance into advocacy, and the deliberate attention to nights and weekends prevented the classic failure mode where off-shifts get blindsided. Staff sentiment through the transition was materially better than prior changes, and the reinforcement period turned initial skepticism into real adoption. Honestly, "nothing dramatic happened" is the win — a smooth, boring go-live is the entire goal of change management.

**Possible limitations:** Good change management is time- and effort-intensive up front, and its payoff is largely invisible — a smooth go-live looks like nothing happened, which makes the investment easy to under-resource next time ("it went fine, so why do we need all that?"). Some resistance is legitimate signal, not noise — occasionally staff push back because the change genuinely has a flaw, and you have to be able to tell the difference rather than steamrolling. And no amount of change management fully eliminates transition stress; it manages and shortens it rather than removing it.

**30-second description:** "A major LIS/middleware upgrade — technically sound, but the real risk was the human transition, where these projects usually fail. We treated it as change management, not just a cutover: engaged respected senior techs on every shift early so they became advocates, communicated the 'why' relentlessly including to nights and weekends, trained role-specifically in a test environment, and ran a deliberate go-live with superusers on the floor and a fallback plan. The go-live was uneventful — TAT dipped briefly and recovered, no error surge — and staff sentiment was far better than prior changes. A boring go-live is the whole point."

**2-minute explanation:** "The upgrade itself was well-scoped technically, but I've learned that major lab system changes almost never fail on the technology — they fail on the human transition. Staff aren't trained well enough, workflows shift without clear communication, night and weekend shifts get less attention than days, resistance builds because people weren't consulted, and go-live becomes chaotic — turnaround time and errors spike exactly when the lab can least afford it, because we can't pause patient care for an upgrade. So I treated this as a change-management project, not just a technical cutover. The single highest-leverage move was engaging the respected senior technologists on each shift early — bringing them into design and testing so they became advocates instead of skeptics, because in a lab the night-shift lead moves more people than any management memo. We communicated the 'why' relentlessly and across every shift, making a specific point of reaching nights and weekends, which are chronically under-communicated to and where go-live failures concentrate. **Interview-friendly phrasing:** "My core belief about lab system changes is that they fail on people, not technology — so the work is engaging the respected senior techs early so they carry the change, communicating the 'why' to every shift including nights, and reinforcing after go-live, not just at it. **Competency demonstrated:** Change management and coalition-building, communication across shifts and cultures, training design, go-live/hypercare planning, and — most relevant to the role — the recognition that multi-site integration *is* recurring change management, making this a strategic capability, not a one-off skill.

If you were a bench-level participant, you can speak from experience: "I lived through an LIS go-live that was managed well and one that wasn't, and the difference was entirely in whether they engaged the senior techs early and reached the off-shifts — which is what I'd carry into any change I helped lead here." That contrast — having seen it done well and badly — is itself a persuasive, honest change-management story.
