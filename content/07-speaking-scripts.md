# Speaking Scripts

These scripts are meant to be *spoken*, not recited. Read them aloud a few times until the ideas feel like yours, then let the exact wording drift — that drift is what makes you sound like a physician thinking in real time rather than a candidate reading a card. On a phone interview, your voice is your only channel: the interviewer cannot see you nod, gesture, or make eye contact, so warmth and pacing carry everything. Slow down more than feels natural. Land on the concrete nouns — "chemistry analyzer," "critical value callback," "LOINC mapping" — because specifics are what signal that you have actually stood in a lab and solved problems. I have marked **[pause]** points where a breath resets the listener and lets a key idea sink in; a short silence after a strong sentence reads as confidence, not hesitation. Keep two or three of these fully internalized (the "tell me about yourself" and "why this role") and treat the rest as flexible frameworks you can assemble on the fly. And leave the **[bracketed placeholders]** as reminders to drop in your own real details — a specific analyzer, a specific project, a specific number — because one true, specific story beats three polished generalities.

---

I'm a clinical pathologist, and what really drives me is the operational side of the lab — making sure the right result gets to the right clinician at the right time, reliably, every single day. **[pause]**

My background is in [your training pathway — e.g., clinical pathology residency plus a chemistry/informatics focus], and along the way I found I gravitate toward the problems that sit at the intersection of medicine, workflow, and systems. I like being the person who can sit with a technologist troubleshooting an analyzer flag, then turn around and talk to IT about why an interface is dropping results, and then explain to a clinician what a result actually means for their patient. **[pause]**

That combination — clinically grounded, operationally aware, comfortable in informatics — is exactly what drew me to a broad clinical pathologist role like this one at Lahey.

## 5. What Pathology Informatics Means to Me

*Approx. 190 spoken words (~78 seconds). Delivery tips: Resist the urge to sound technical for its own sake — the strongest version of this answer is plain-spoken and patient-centered. Use one standard (LOINC, HL7) as an anchor so it's clear you know the vocabulary, but don't drown the answer in acronyms.*

To me, pathology informatics is really about one thing: making sure that information about a patient is accurate, complete, and usable at the moment a clinician needs it. **[pause]** Everything else is in service of that.

I tend to think of it in a few layers. There's the fundamentals — how a result is represented, coded, and given meaning, which is where standards like LOINC, SNOMED, and units in UCUM come in. There's the systems layer — the LIS, the middleware, the interfaces, and how they talk to the EHR over HL7. There's the workflow layer — how a specimen actually moves and how a result actually reaches a human being. And there's the governance layer — who owns the data, who validates changes, and how we keep all of it safe and consistent. **[pause]**

What I care about is that these aren't abstract IT concerns. A mismapped LOINC code or a broken interface isn't a technical footnote — it can mean a clinician sees the wrong value or no value at all. **[pause]** So for me, informatics is applied patient safety. It's clinical work that happens to be expressed through systems.

## 6. Why Core Laboratory Management Interests Me

*Approx. 180 spoken words (~72 seconds). Delivery tips: Let some genuine enthusiasm show — management can sound like a chore, so signal that you actually enjoy it. Ground it in a reliability metaphor (the lab as a system that has to perform every day) rather than in org-chart language.*

Core lab management interests me because it's where medicine meets reliability engineering. **[pause]** The core lab is high-volume, it never stops, and the tolerance for error is very low — a chemistry line running slow at 2 a.m. is a patient-care problem, not just a throughput number on a dashboard.

I like the discipline of that. Thinking about turnaround time, instrument uptime, QC and calibration, staffing and competency, reagent and supply chain, and how all of those interact to produce a result that a clinician can trust. **[pause]** It's genuinely systems thinking — you can't fix turnaround by pushing people harder; you fix it by understanding where the process actually breaks and redesigning that piece.

And I find it deeply satisfying because the wins are concrete. When you shave twenty minutes off a critical result, or you cut a recurring instrument downtime, you can feel the effect downstream — in the ED, on the floors, in patient throughput. **[pause]** It's operational work with a direct clinical payoff, and that's exactly the kind of impact I want to have. [Mention one management area you're especially drawn to — e.g., POCT oversight, QC redesign.]

## 7. How I Approach Quality and Safety

*Approx. 195 spoken words (~80 seconds). Delivery tips: This is where you sound like a grown-up leader — steady, non-defensive, systems-oriented. When you hit the "blame the system, not the person" idea, slow down; it's a signal of maturity and it's what good lab directors actually believe.*

My core belief about quality and safety is that when something goes wrong, the first question is almost never "who made the mistake" — it's "what let this mistake happen." **[pause]** Good, careful people make errors when the system allows them to. So my instinct is always to look at the process, not the person.

Practically, that means I lean on a few things. Standardization, so the safe way is the easy way and the default. Real QC — not just running controls to check a box, but actually watching for shifts and trends before they become misreported patient results. And building checks into the workflow itself, so the system catches the error rather than relying on someone to catch it under pressure. **[pause]**

I'm also a believer in the boring, unglamorous parts — good documentation, clear SOPs, competency assessment, and honest root-cause analysis when something does slip. When there's an event, I want a blameless review that actually changes the process, so the same thing can't recur. **[pause]**

And I try to keep it patient-centered. Every quality control we run and every validation we do is ultimately protecting a real person who will never know we caught it. That framing keeps the work from feeling like bureaucracy.

## 8. How I Work with Technologists, Clinicians, and IT

*Approx. 200 spoken words (~82 seconds). Delivery tips: This answer is fundamentally about respect — let that come through in tone. Give each group its own sentence of genuine credit; interviewers notice when you speak about technologists as partners rather than subordinates.*

I think of the lab as a team where I happen to sit at a useful crossroads, and my job is often to translate between groups that don't naturally speak the same language. **[pause]**

With technologists, my first move is always to listen, because they know the bench better than anyone. They're usually the first to notice when something's drifting — an analyzer acting up, a reagent lot behaving oddly, a workflow that's quietly become a workaround. I treat them as the sensors of the whole operation, and I want them comfortable escalating early, not waiting until it's a crisis. **[pause]**

With clinicians, my job is to make the lab legible — to help them understand what a result means, when to trust it, and when to call me. I'd much rather get a "does this make sense?" phone call than have a result misinterpreted in silence. **[pause]**

And with IT, the key is that I can speak both languages. I can take a clinical requirement and turn it into something concrete an interface analyst can build, and I can take a technical constraint and explain to clinicians why it matters. **[pause]** Most failures in a lab happen at the seams between these groups, so I see my role as stitching those seams — making sure nothing falls through the gaps between the bench, the clinician, and the system.

*Approx. 195 spoken words (~80 seconds). Delivery tips: This is your chance to show you understand BILH's actual near-term challenge. Keep it concrete — name the real harmonization problems (reference ranges, test codes) rather than talking about "synergy." End on patient safety so it doesn't sound like pure logistics.*

The concrete problems are things like: do the same test names mean the same thing at every site? Are the reference ranges aligned, or is a "normal" potassium at one hospital flagged as abnormal at another? Are we mapping to LOINC consistently so a result means the same thing no matter where it was run? Are result comments, units, and critical-value thresholds standardized? **[pause]** Those sound like small details, but they're precisely where patient safety lives when you integrate.

My approach is to start from the clinical end and work backward. What does a clinician need to see, and how do we make it consistent and trustworthy across the network? Then you deal with the interfaces, the mappings, and the governance underneath. **[pause]**

And I'm a strong believer that integration needs governance, not just plumbing — someone has to own the decisions about how tests are harmonized, and that's a role I'd genuinely enjoy contributing to. **[pause]** Done well, integration means a patient gets the same quality of result whether they're seen at one BILH hospital or another. That's the goal worth aiming at. [Insert a real integration or harmonization example if you have one.]

## 10. How I Handle Operational Problems in the Lab

*Approx. 190 spoken words (~78 seconds). Delivery tips: Show a calm, sequential method — interviewers want to hear that you don't panic and don't jump to blame. Walk through your steps out loud; the structure itself signals competence. Use a real example if you have one ready.*

When something breaks operationally, I try to be calm and methodical rather than reactive, because the first instinct in a crisis is usually to do *something*, and that's not always the right something. **[pause]**

My first move is to contain the impact and protect patient care — if results are affected, what's the safe fallback? Do we reroute to a backup instrument, put out a service notice, switch to a manual process? Stabilize first. **[pause]**

Then I want to actually understand the problem before I fix it. I go to the bench and the people closest to it, because the story on the ground is almost always richer than the story on the dashboard. I try to separate the symptom — "turnaround is slow" — from the actual cause, which might be an interface backlog, a staffing gap, a QC failure, or a reagent issue. **[pause]**

Then it's a fix, but a durable one. I care a lot about whether the fix prevents recurrence, not just whether it clears today's fire. So after we resolve it, I want a real look at root cause and a change to the process so it can't quietly come back. **[pause]**

For example, [insert a real operational problem you solved and how you made the fix stick]. The through-line for me is always: stabilize, understand, fix, and then harden so it doesn't happen again.
